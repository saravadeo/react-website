## Anthropic's Mythos Scares Wall Street: Powell and Bessent Summon Bank CEOs

**Anthropic's Mythos model discovered decades-old cybersecurity vulnerabilities that no human had ever found — and the fallout was immediate. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon called Mythos's capabilities "a real issue," Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Fed Chair Jerome Powell urgently convened a meeting with every major Wall Street bank CEO to discuss the cyber risks, and Anthropic agreed to share Mythos's threat findings with global finance watchdogs.**

### What's happening

- Anthropic's Mythos model found cybersecurity vulnerabilities dating back 27 years that no human security researcher had ever discovered
- JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon publicly warned that Mythos reveals "a lot more vulnerabilities" for cyberattacks and called it "a real issue"
- Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Fed Chair Jerome Powell urgently summoned every major US bank CEO to discuss Mythos's cybersecurity implications
- Anthropic has agreed to share Mythos's cyber threat findings with global financial watchdogs
- Fortune reported that the discovery "forced an emergency meeting with every major Wall Street CEO"
- The Wall Street Journal reported that Anthropic is letting Mythos users share cyber threat intelligence with others
- The New York Times reported that banks were formally warned about Anthropic's new, powerful AI technology

### Why it matters

This is the moment AI went from "interesting security tool" to "systemic financial risk." When the most powerful AI model in the world starts finding vulnerabilities that have existed for nearly three decades without anyone noticing, it doesn't just demonstrate capability — it reveals how much hidden danger was always there, waiting to be found. Dimon's warning wasn't just about what Mythos can do; it was about what happens when this capability becomes widely available. If Anthropic's model can find these flaws, other models — including adversarial ones — can too. The emergency meeting with bank CEOs signals that Washington sees this as a systemic risk, not just a tech curiosity. And Anthropic's decision to share threat intelligence with regulators is a significant pivot toward being seen as a responsible actor — but it also raises uncomfortable questions about who gets access to this kind of power and under what conditions. The financial system is built on trust, and AI that can find hidden cracks in that trust is both its best defender and its most terrifying adversary.

## Thinking Machines Lab Releases Inkling: A 975B Open-Weights Model Built for Customization

**Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab released Inkling, its first AI model — a 975B-parameter open-weights multimodal Mixture-of-Experts model with 41B active parameters and controllable thinking effort. Inkling is designed specifically for fine-tuning and enterprise customization, positioning it as a counterweight to the one-size-fits-all approach of frontier models.**

### What's happening

- Thinking Machines Lab, founded by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati, released Inkling — its first model and a direct entry into the open-weights arena
- Inkling is a 975B-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model with 41B active parameters, making it the new leading US open-weights model
- The model features controllable thinking effort, allowing users to dial reasoning up or down depending on the task
- Inkling is multimodal, handling both text and images
- Thinking Machines emphasizes two design principles: low cost of deployment and "resistance to censorship" — a direct appeal to users frustrated with frontier model guardrails
- The model is available on Databricks and designed for fine-tuning, letting enterprises customize it for their specific needs
- VentureBeat notes the model is "focused on low cost and resistance to censorship"
- TechCrunch describes it as Thinking Machines' bet "against one-size-fits-all AI"
- WIRED calls it a significant milestone for the startup, which has been building in relative secrecy since Murati left OpenAI

### Why it matters

Mira Murati was the CTO of OpenAI — she oversaw the development of GPT-4, DALL-E, and ChatGPT itself. Now she's building the anti-OpenAI: open weights, customizable, censorship-resistant. Inkling's 975B parameters make it one of the largest open models available, and its Mixture-of-Experts architecture means it can run efficiently by only activating 41B parameters at a time. The "controllable thinking effort" feature is particularly interesting — it's a direct response to the complaint that reasoning models are either overthinking simple questions or underthinking hard ones. But the real signal is the framing: "resistance to censorship" is a loaded phrase in 2026. It's a bet that there's a growing market of users and enterprises who want powerful AI without the content guardrails that Anthropic and OpenAI impose. Whether that's liberating or dangerous depends on your perspective, but it's clearly a deliberate market positioning against the frontier model companies. The open-weights war is heating up, and Thinking Machines just became a major combatant.

## OpenAI Unleashes GPT-Red: An AI That Attacks Its Own Models

**OpenAI revealed GPT-Red, an AI red-teaming system specifically designed to attack OpenAI's own models to find vulnerabilities. GPT-Red outperformed human red teamers on prompt injection tests and found security flaws that human testers had missed — including vulnerabilities used to strengthen GPT-5.6.**

### What's happening

- OpenAI detailed GPT-Red, an AI system built to attack its own models as a red-teaming tool
- GPT-Red beat human red teamers on prompt injection benchmarks
- The system found vulnerabilities that human testers had never discovered, which were then used to harden GPT-5.6
- MIT Technology Review profiles GPT-Red as "an LLM super-hacker OpenAI built to make its models safer"
- OpenAI is using the findings to strengthen GPT-5.6 specifically against prompt injection attacks
- Help Net Security reports that GPT-Red "beat human red teamers on a prompt injection test"
- SiliconANGLE describes it as "an AI that attacks its own models to find flaws"
- OpenAI also launched a new initiative to help find and patch open source bugs

### Why it matters

Using AI to attack AI is one of those ideas that sounds obvious in retrospect but is genuinely transformative in practice. Human red teamers are slow, expensive, and limited by their own experience. GPT-Red can probe attack surfaces at machine speed and scale, finding vulnerabilities that humans either missed or couldn't conceive of. The fact that it outperformed humans on prompt injection — the most common and dangerous attack vector for LLMs — is a significant milestone. But the deeper implication is about the arms race: if OpenAI can build GPT-Red, so can adversaries. The same AI red-teaming capability that strengthens GPT-5.6 could theoretically be used to find zero-days in any model. OpenAI is essentially proving that AI security requires AI — and that the defensive and offensive uses of this technology are two sides of the same coin. The open source bug initiative is a nice complement, but GPT-Red is the real story: the age of AI-vs-AI security has officially begun.

## China Drafts a $295 Billion AI Buildout — Locking Out Nvidia and Mandating 80% Domestic Chips

**China has drafted a $295 billion plan to build a national AI data center grid over five years, mandating that 80% of the chips must be domestically produced. The plan, if executed, would be the largest single AI infrastructure investment in history — and would effectively lock Nvidia, AMD, and other Western chipmakers out of the world's second-largest AI market.**

### What's happening

- China has drafted a $295 billion plan to build a nationwide AI data center grid
- The plan mandates that 80% of chips used must be domestically produced, targeting a 2028 completion timeline
- Bloomberg first reported the plan, which would be the largest AI infrastructure investment ever
- The mandate effectively locks out Nvidia and AMD from China's AI infrastructure buildout
- Tom's Hardware reports the plan could "run into limits of local chip production" — China's domestic chip industry may not be able to meet the 80% target
- Reuters confirmed the $295 billion figure
- Tech Times reports China's "AI Data Center Grid Locks Out Nvidia With $295 Billion Domestic Chip Mandate"
- Meanwhile, CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies), China's DRAM champion, aims to raise $8.6 billion in what would be Asia's biggest IPO of 2026 so far — with some reports citing figures as high as $9.8 billion
- A Bloomberg investigation also revealed CXMT as a "secretive chipmaker built to dodge US curbs"
- A co-founder of a tech company was charged with diverting $2.5 billion in Nvidia AI chips to China in violation of export laws

### Why it matters

This is the clearest signal yet that the US-China tech decoupling is accelerating — and it's not just about export controls anymore. China isn't just trying to work around US chip restrictions; it's actively designing a parallel AI infrastructure that excludes American companies entirely. The $295 billion figure is staggering — it's roughly equivalent to the entire GDP of Portugal, committed to building a sovereign AI compute grid. The 80% domestic chip mandate is ambitious but also the plan's biggest vulnerability: China's domestic chip industry, while growing fast, is still years behind Nvidia in AI training performance. CXMT's massive IPO and the $2.5 billion chip smuggling scheme are two sides of the same coin — China is both investing in domestic capability and still finding ways to access Western chips when it needs to. The question isn't whether China will build AI infrastructure at this scale (it will), but whether domestic chips can deliver competitive performance by 2028. If they can, the global AI landscape splits into two incompatible ecosystems. If they can't, this $295 billion bet could become a very expensive lesson in the limits of industrial policy.

## Perplexity Launches "Space" — A Secure Sandbox for AI Agents

**Perplexity AI launched Space, a secure sandbox environment for AI agents that can safely execute code, browse the web, and interact with systems without risking the host machine. The launch comes as Perplexity also released its "Personal Computer" — a Mac mini running an AI OS — and expanded its agentic API offerings.**

### What's happening

- Perplexity launched Space, a secure sandbox for AI agents to run code and interact with systems safely
- SiliconANGLE describes it as designed "to make its AI agents secure and powerful"
- AI Business calls it "Space Sandbox for Agents"
- Perplexity also recently released its "Personal Computer" — a Mac mini running an AI OS that brings Perplexity's agents to a physical device
- The company expanded its APIs to give developers access to agentic workflows and orchestration
- The sandbox is positioned as solving one of the biggest problems in agentic AI: how to let agents take actions safely

### Why it matters

The agentic AI problem isn't just about making agents smart — it's about making them safe. When an AI agent can execute code, browse websites, and interact with external systems, the risk of catastrophic mistakes skyrockets. Perplexity's Space sandbox is an elegant solution: give agents a contained environment where they can act freely without endangering the host system. It's the same principle as browser sandboxes, but applied to AI agents. The timing is strategic — Perplexity is betting big on agentic AI, and Space gives developers the safety infrastructure they need to actually deploy agents in production. Combined with the Personal Computer hardware and the expanded APIs, Perplexity is building a full-stack agentic platform: the models, the runtime, the sandbox, and the hardware. Whether this integrated approach wins against more specialized competitors remains to be seen, but the sandbox problem is real, and Perplexity is one of the first to solve it properly.
