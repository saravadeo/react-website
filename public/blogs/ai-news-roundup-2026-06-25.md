## OpenAI Unveils Jalapeño — Its First Custom AI Inference Chip, Built with Broadcom

**OpenAI has officially revealed its first custom AI inference chip, named Jalapeño**, designed in partnership with Broadcom specifically for the unique needs of OpenAI's inference systems.

### What's happening

- The chip was designed from the ground up for OpenAI's inference workload, not training — a crucial distinction that separates it from Nvidia's general-purpose GPUs
- Broadcom, the chip design company that also builds custom silicon for Google and other hyperscalers, partnered on the design
- This is OpenAI's first step toward reducing its dependency on Nvidia's GPU monopoly, which has been the single biggest cost driver in the company's multi-billion-dollar compute budget
- The announcement comes amid a broader industry shift where every major AI lab — Google, Amazon, Microsoft — is designing its own custom silicon

### Why it matters

OpenAI spending billions on Nvidia GPUs was always going to be a temporary arrangement. Jalapeño is the first public signal that OpenAI is serious about vertical integration — controlling the full stack from silicon to model to product. If the chip delivers on its inference performance promises, it could dramatically reduce OpenAI's per-query costs and give them a structural advantage over competitors who are still paying Nvidia's premium. But custom silicon is notoriously hard to get right — just ask Google, which took multiple Tensor Processing Unit generations before it had something competitive. OpenAI is betting it can learn faster than its rivals did, and with $30B+ in funding, it has the runway to try.

## Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of a Brazen Campaign to Extract Claude's Model Capabilities

**Anthropic has publicly accused Alibaba of running a systematic campaign to illicitly extract capabilities from its Claude AI models**, marking one of the most direct corporate confrontations in the US-China AI rivalry.

### What's happening

- Anthropic released a detailed statement alleging that Alibaba used a variety of techniques — from mass prompt engineering to sophisticated extraction attacks — to reverse-engineer Claude's model capabilities
- CNBC reported that Anthropic called the campaign "brazen" and "illicit," suggesting the extraction went far beyond typical model distillation or learning from outputs
- The accusation comes at a particularly sensitive time: Anthropic's most powerful models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, are already under US export controls that bar foreign nationals from accessing them
- China's 360 Security Technology claimed it has developed tools to match Anthropic's Mythos capabilities, raising questions about whether knowledge transfer has already occurred

### Why it matters

This isn't just corporate rivalry — it's the AI equivalent of accusing a rival nation of industrial espionage. Anthropic's models represent billions of dollars in R&D, and if Alibaba can replicate their capabilities without that investment, the entire economics of frontier AI development collapse. The accusation also puts enormous pressure on US policymakers: export controls on Mythos were already controversial, and if Chinese companies can extract model capabilities through other means, those controls may be fighting the last war. For the broader AI industry, this is a warning shot — your model weights and capabilities are your crown jewels, and protecting them is now a first-class engineering problem, not an afterthought.

## Google Ships Computer Use in Gemini 3.5 Flash

**Google has introduced built-in computer use capabilities in Gemini 3.5 Flash**, enabling the model to directly interact with desktop applications, click buttons, navigate interfaces, and perform multi-step tasks autonomously.

### What's happening

- The feature allows Gemini 3.5 Flash to see a computer screen, understand UI elements, and take actions like clicking, typing, and navigating — similar to what Anthropic launched with Claude's computer use last year
- This is a significant upgrade to Gemini 3.5 Flash specifically, Google's fast and cost-efficient model, suggesting the company wants computer use to be a mainstream capability, not just a research demo
- The announcement positions Google directly against Anthropic's Claude computer use feature and Microsoft's evolving Copilot capabilities
- The blog post emphasizes the built-in nature of the tool — meaning developers don't need to construct complex agent pipelines to get computer use working

### Why it matters

Computer use is the gateway drug to true AI agents. Once a model can reliably navigate a GUI, fill in forms, and chain together multi-step workflows, the range of tasks AI can handle explodes — from booking flights to managing spreadsheets to doing your entire morning routine of email triage. By putting this in Flash (the cheaper, faster model) rather than Pro or Ultra, Google is signaling that computer use shouldn't be a premium feature. It should be table stakes. The race to make AI agents that can actually *do things* — not just talk about doing things — just got a lot more competitive.

## Qualcomm Acquires Modular for $4 Billion in AI Software Push

**Qualcomm is acquiring AI infrastructure startup Modular for $4 billion**, in one of the largest acquisitions in the AI tooling space.

### What's happening

- Modular built a popular AI infrastructure platform including the Mojo programming language, designed to make AI development and deployment faster and more efficient
- The acquisition gives Qualcomm a significant software layer to complement its hardware ambitions — the company wants to be the AI compute platform from phone to data center
- Reuters reported that Qualcomm has also named Meta as the first major customer for its new AI data center CPU, signaling that the chipmaker is serious about competing with Nvidia and AMD in the server market
- The deal comes as Qualcomm pivots from its mobile-centric business toward being a full-stack AI compute company

### Why it matters

Qualcomm isn't just buying a company — it's buying a narrative. For years, Qualcomm was "the Snapdragon company," making chips for phones. With Modular, it gets an AI-native software stack. With Meta as a data center customer, it gets enterprise credibility. And with its new data center CPUs, it gets a seat at the table with Nvidia, AMD, and the custom-silicon efforts of Google and Amazon. The $4 billion price tag for Modular isn't cheap, but if Qualcomm can tie together hardware, software, and developer tools into a coherent AI platform, it could be the bargain of the decade. The alternative — staying a mobile chip company while AI eats the world — wasn't really an alternative at all.

## SpaceX Signs $6.3 Billion Compute Deal with Reflection AI

**SpaceX has signed a computing power deal with open-source AI startup Reflection worth up to $6.3 billion**, in what may be the largest single AI infrastructure contract ever disclosed.

### What's happening

- Reflection AI is an open-source AI startup focused on building autonomous agents — AI systems that can reason, plan, and execute complex multi-step tasks without human intervention
- CNBC reported that the deal is structured as a capacity agreement, with SpaceX providing compute and Reflection providing its agent technology
- The $6.3 billion figure puts Reflection on the map as one of the best-funded AI startups, despite its open-source focus
- SpaceX's interest in AI agents is logical: autonomous systems are critical for rocket operations, satellite management, and eventually Mars missions

### Why it matters

A $6.3 billion compute deal is staggering — that's more than most AI startups' entire valuations. But what makes this deal interesting isn't just the money; it's the alignment. SpaceX needs autonomous systems that can operate reliably in extreme environments where human response times are too slow and communication delays make remote control impossible. Reflection's open-source agent technology could be exactly what SpaceX needs for next-generation space operations. If the deal delivers, it could prove that the most valuable AI applications aren't in chatbots or image generators — they're in autonomous systems that make real decisions in the physical world.

## US Pressures Meta to Accept AI Security Reviews as National Security Concerns Rise

**The US government is pressuring Meta to agree to formal AI security reviews**, according to the New York Times, as national security concerns about powerful AI models continue to escalate.

### What's happening

- The push comes in the context of broader US efforts to control the spread of advanced AI capabilities, particularly to foreign adversaries
- Meta has been resistant to some forms of government oversight, arguing that its open-source approach to AI (through the Llama model family) promotes safety through transparency
- The request for security reviews follows the Trump administration's order giving OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic 30 days to report new frontier models to the government
- The New York Times report notes that Meta's open-weight models could potentially be fine-tuned by foreign actors for malicious purposes, creating a unique security challenge

### Why it matters

Meta is the outlier in the frontier AI race — the only major player committed to releasing open-weight models. That's great for researchers and developers, but it's a nightmare for security hawks who worry about who can access and modify these models. If the US government forces Meta to submit to security reviews, it could fundamentally reshape the open-source AI movement. Meta might be forced to add restrictions, delay releases, or even change its open-weight strategy entirely. The tension between openness and security isn't new, but in the age of Mythos-level capabilities, the stakes have never been higher.

## GLM-5.2 Arrives as a Step Change for Open Agents

**Zhipu AI's GLM-5.2 is being described as a "step change for open agents"**, according to analysis from Interconnects, with significant improvements in tool use, reasoning, and autonomous task completion.

### What's happening

- GLM-5.2 is the latest model from Zhipu AI, one of China's leading AI labs, and it shows major improvements in agentic capabilities — the ability to autonomously plan and execute multi-step tasks
- The model is open-weight, making it accessible for researchers and developers who want to build on top of it
- Early benchmarks show GLM-5.2 narrowing the gap with frontier models from OpenAI and Anthropic on agentic tasks
- The release comes amid increasing US-China tensions over AI capabilities, with Anthropic's accusations against Alibaba highlighting the complexity of model security

### Why it matters

GLM-5.2 is proof that the open-source AI movement isn't limited to Western labs. Zhipu's model is competitive on agentic tasks — exactly the capability that everyone from Anthropic to OpenAI to Google is racing to perfect. If open-weight models can match frontier capabilities, the economics of the entire AI industry shift. Why pay premium API prices when a locally-run model can do the job? Of course, that's exactly what US security hawks are worried about — and why the debate over open-weight models is about to get much more heated.
