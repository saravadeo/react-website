## SpaceXAI Launches Grok 4.5: Cursor-Trained, Opus-Class, and Half the Price

**SpaceXAI released Grok 4.5, a new model trained in partnership with Cursor for coding, agentic tasks, and knowledge work. Elon Musk described it as an "Opus-class model" comparing it to Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8. It serves at 80 tokens per second and costs $2/$6 per million tokens — dramatically undercutting rivals.**

### What's happening

- SpaceXAI launched Grok 4.5, the latest iteration of the Grok model family, on July 8
- The model was co-trained with Cursor, the popular AI coding assistant, giving it strong coding and agentic capabilities
- Elon Musk called it an "Opus-class model," benchmarking it against Claude Opus 4.8 — though skeptics noted Opus is Anthropic's older model family
- Pricing is aggressive: $2 per million input tokens and $6 per million output tokens, roughly half the cost of comparable models from Anthropic and OpenAI
- The model serves at 80 tokens per second, making it one of the faster frontier models available
- Grok 4.5 ranked #1 on Harvey's Legal Agent Benchmark, a notable result for a model positioned primarily for coding tasks
- Available on Grok Build and APIs immediately, with broader public access rolling out after strong beta feedback

### Why it matters

This is SpaceXAI's clearest signal yet that it intends to compete on price, not just performance. The Cursor partnership is clever — it gives Grok 4.5 a built-in distribution channel through one of the most popular developer tools. But calling it "Opus-class" when Anthropic is on Opus 4.8 is like calling your car "Porsche-class" because it goes as fast as a 2019 model. The real story is the pricing: at $2/$6M tokens, SpaceXAI is willing to lose money on inference to gain market share, the same playbook that disrupted rockets and satellite internet. If coding agents are the next battleground, Grok 4.5 just landed with both feet.

## Beijing Considers Restricting Overseas Access to China's Top AI Models

**Reuters reported that Beijing is looking at curbing overseas access to China's most capable AI models, just as U.S. lawmakers are investigating the growing use of Chinese AI systems by American companies. The dual squeeze from both governments could reshape the global AI landscape.**

### What's happening

- Reuters exclusively reported that Beijing is considering restrictions on overseas access to China's top AI models, according to sources familiar with the matter
- The potential restrictions come as Chinese models like DeepSeek and others have been gaining significant global market share — hitting 46% of OpenRouter traffic at peak
- Simultaneously, U.S. lawmakers are probing the growing use of Chinese AI models in American companies, raising national security concerns
- CNBC reported that Congress is investigating whether Chinese AI models pose data privacy or security risks when used by U.S. firms
- The moves from both governments threaten to create a bifurcated global AI ecosystem, with Chinese models restricted from Western markets and vice versa
- This follows DeepSeek's reported plans to design its own inference chip and raise $7 billion, reducing dependence on foreign semiconductor technology

### Why it matters

We're watching the Iron Curtain descend on AI in real time. Beijing restricting overseas model access would hurt Western developers who've been relying on cheaper Chinese alternatives, while U.S. scrutiny of Chinese models in American companies creates pressure from the other side. The irony is thick: Chinese models gained market share precisely because they were cheaper and "good enough," and now both governments want to cut that equation off. The losers are developers who built on these models and companies that integrated them into production systems. The winners? Domestic model providers in both markets who'll face less competition. If you thought AI was going to be a global, open technology, the geopolitics disagree.

## Nvidia Loses $1 Trillion in Market Cap as Valuation Hits Pre-AI Boom Levels

**Nvidia's stock has plunged so far that its valuation has dropped to levels not seen since before the AI boom began. The chipmaker has lost approximately $1 trillion in market capitalization, and analysts are split on whether this is a buying opportunity or the start of a deeper reckoning.**

### What's happening

- Nvidia has lost roughly $1 trillion in market capitalization from its peak, with the stock declining 16% in recent weeks
- The company's price-to-earnings ratio has hit a multi-year low, with some analysts calling it the cheapest valuation since 2019
- The decline comes amid broader "AI spending jitters" — the Magnificent 7 tech stocks collectively shed $2.3 trillion in value in June alone
- NVIDIA's Kyber chip architecture reportedly faced delays, adding to investor concerns about near-term growth
- Morningstar and other analysts see "compelling value" at current levels, while Apollo warned that AI profits are "a no-show" outside the tech sector
- The company continues to set revenue records, but the market is pricing in slower growth and questioning whether AI infrastructure spending is sustainable

### Why it matters

The market is having a serious "show me" moment with AI. Nvidia is still printing money — revenue records keep breaking — but the stock market has stopped believing in the hockey stick. When your P/E ratio drops back to 2019 levels despite record revenue, it means investors are pricing in the possibility that the AI infrastructure gold rush has a ceiling. The Apollo warning is particularly sharp: outside of tech companies, AI profits remain largely theoretical. The question isn't whether AI works; it's whether enough people will pay enough money for enough long enough to justify the trillions in capex. Nvidia's $1 trillion slide is the market's way of saying: "We believed. Now prove it."

## Meta Launches Muse Image: AI Image Generation Using Your Instagram Photos (Opt-Out Only)

**Meta unveiled Muse Image, an AI image generator that lets users create images using content from public Instagram profiles. The feature launched across Meta AI, Instagram, and WhatsApp — with an opt-out mechanism that many users found inadequate.**

### What's happening

- Meta launched Muse Image (alongside Muse Video) on July 7, integrating AI image generation directly into Meta AI, Instagram, and WhatsApp
- The tool can generate images based on any public Instagram profile's visual content — meaning your photos can be used by strangers to create AI images in your style
- Users can opt out, but the process requires navigating Meta's settings and there's no global opt-out toggle
- BBC and TechCrunch both reported significant user pushback over the use of personal photos for AI training without explicit consent
- Meta's approach differs from competitors by leveraging its massive existing content library rather than licensing external training data
- The opt-out design means Meta trains on your photos by default, requiring users to actively prevent it — the same playbook Google used this week with search uploads for AI training

### Why it matters

Meta just did the thing everyone was afraid of: it turned billions of Instagram photos into a proprietary AI training dataset without asking. The opt-out model is a deliberate choice — Meta knows most people won't bother, so they'll get 99%+ participation by default. It's the same move Google pulled this week when it started using search uploads for AI training. The message from Big Tech is consistent: your data is our data, unless you say otherwise, and we're making it hard to say otherwise. The real question is whether regulators will finally force a shift to opt-in, or whether "we added a settings toggle" will continue to count as consent.

## Illinois Governor Signs First-in-Nation AI Regulation Bill Requiring Third-Party Safety Audits

**Governor JB Pritzker signed a landmark AI regulation bill into law, making Illinois the first state to require third-party safety audits for major AI companies. The law targets "the tech bros" and sets a national standard for AI governance.**

### What's happening

- Governor JB Pritzker signed the Illinois AI regulation bill on July 7, making it the first state law requiring third-party safety audits for AI companies
- Pritzker explicitly said the law aims to "rein in the tech bros," signaling an adversarial stance toward unregulated AI development
- The bill requires major AI companies to submit to independent safety audits — a first at the state level in the U.S.
- Multiple outlets reported this as a "landmark" and "first-in-nation" law that could set a template for other states
- The law also addresses AI-generated bullying, reflecting growing concern about AI misuse in education and social contexts
- This comes alongside a new California initiative to monitor and track AI's impacts on the workforce
- The EU simultaneously unveiled its own AI cybersecurity action plan, noting that only 30 of 3,000 AI systems in Europe are European-made, and none meet EU standards

### Why it matters

For years, the AI industry's argument against regulation was "we'll regulate ourselves." Illinois just called that bluff. Third-party safety audits are the bare minimum for an industry building systems that can generate misinformation at scale, automate discrimination, and — per the Five Eyes intelligence alliance — potentially launch "devastating attacks on governments." The tech industry will push back hard, arguing that audits will slow innovation and that state-level regulation creates a patchwork nightmare. But Pritzker's move reflects a growing political consensus that self-regulation has failed. If other states follow Illinois's lead (and California's workforce monitoring suggests they will), the AI industry's regulatory holiday is over.

Anthropic expanded Claude Cowork — its enterprise AI assistant — to web and mobile platforms. The move signals Anthropic's push beyond developer tools into the broader enterprise market, directly competing with Microsoft Copilot and Google's Gemini Workspace integrations.

### Zuckerberg Admits AI Agents Haven't Progressed as Fast as Hoped
Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg told staff in an internal town hall that AI agent technology "hasn't progressed as quickly as we'd hoped." The admission came after Meta laid off 8,000 employees as part of its AI reorganization. Zuckerberg's candor is notable: the industry has been promising autonomous AI agents "any day now" for over a year, and even the CEO of one of the biggest AI investors is acknowledging the gap between demos and reality.

### EU Unveils AI Cybersecurity Action Plan
The European Commission released a comprehensive AI cybersecurity action plan, warning that only 30 of 3,000 AI systems in the EU are European-made, and none meet the bloc's own standards. The plan aims to reduce dependence on U.S. and Chinese AI systems while addressing AI-driven cybersecurity threats. It's a sobering acknowledgment that Europe's regulatory leadership hasn't translated into AI sovereignty.

### Google Starts Using Your Search Uploads for AI Training (Opt-Out Only)
Google quietly began using photos and files uploaded to Google Search for AI training, with an opt-out mechanism that most users won't find. TechCrunch, WIRED, and Computerworld all published guides on how to opt out. The move mirrors Meta's approach: train on user data by default, make opting out possible but obscure.

### Anthropic's Five Major Hires From OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and xAI
