# AI News Roundup — June 4, 2026

The AI industry isn't slowing down. This week, Elon Musk unveiled a dramatically restructured xAI after folding it into SpaceX, Google pulled off a stunning $2.4 billion acquihire of Windsurf's top talent right out from under OpenAI, SoftBank placed an $87.3 billion bet on European AI infrastructure, and Chinese startup MiniMax launched an open-weight coding model claiming frontier-level performance. ## 🚀 xAI Restructures Into Four Units After SpaceX Merger

Elon Musk held xAI's first all-hands meeting since the company merged with SpaceX on May 6, and the message was clear: the startup is being rebuilt from the ground up. The new xAI — now technically the **SpaceXAI division** — is organized into four core teams:

### The Four Units

- **Grok**: The chatbot and voice assistant unit, continuing development of the Grok conversational AI that competes with ChatGPT and Claude.
- **Coding**: A dedicated unit focused on AI-powered code generation and developer tools — putting xAI in direct competition with GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and Claude Code.
- **Imagine**: The image and video generation team, responsible for Grok Imagine and visual AI capabilities.
- **Macro Hard**: Perhaps the most ambitious unit, focused on building **AI agents that can emulate entire companies** — autonomous systems that handle workflows across organizations without human oversight.

### The Exodus

The restructuring wasn't bloodless. **Six of xAI's twelve founding members have now departed**, including co-founders Jimmy Ba and Yuhuai Wu, who left during the transition. Earlier departures included Igor Babuschkin, Kyle Kosic, Christian Szegedy, and Greg Yang. Musk framed the departures as inevitable: *"As a company grows, especially as quickly as xAI, the structure must evolve."*

### The Bigger Vision

Musk outlined an expansive vision during the all-hands: combining X (social media data), xAI (models), and SpaceX (orbital infrastructure) into **one vertically integrated AI and infrastructure stack**. He even floated the idea of **space-based data centers** powered by SpaceX rockets — a concept that sounds like science fiction but reflects the company's ambition to scale AI "beyond Earth's limits."

### Why It Matters

The restructuring signals that Musk sees xAI not as a standalone chatbot company but as the intelligence layer of the SpaceX empire. The Macro Hard unit in particular — building AI agents that can run entire business functions — positions xAI against Anthropic, Microsoft, and every other company betting on agentic AI. Whether the unit can deliver on that promise with half its founding team gone is the open question.

## 💰 Google's $2.4B Windsurf Acquihire: A Talent Heist That Left OpenAI Empty-Handed

Google pulled off one of the most dramatic moves in the AI talent wars this week, securing **Windsurf's CEO Varun Mohan and approximately 42 top engineers** in a $2.4 billion deal that also includes a **non-exclusive license to Windsurf's AI coding technology**.

### How It Happened

Windsurf (formerly Codeium) had been in **advanced acquisition talks with OpenAI** for a deal valued at around $3 billion, according to multiple reports. But Google swooped in with a deal that offered Windsurf's leadership better terms — and crucially, avoided the regulatory scrutiny that a full acquisition would trigger.

### The Deal Structure

Unlike a traditional acquisition, Google structured this as an **acquihire plus licensing deal**:

- **$2.4 billion** in total: licensing fees for Windsurf's technology and compensation for the incoming team
- **Non-exclusive license**: Google gets access to Windsurf's IP, but Windsurf retains the ability to license its tech to others
- **Key talent**: CEO Varun Mohan and senior R&D employees join **Google DeepMind** to work on **agentic coding initiatives** within the Gemini project

This mirrors Google's 2024 deal with Character.AI, where the company paid $2.7 billion to re-hire Noam Shazeer and bring on key staff through a similar licensing structure.

### The Fallout

The deal is a significant blow to OpenAI, which has been racing to build out its coding assistant capabilities. But the impact extends beyond the two companies: many Windsurf employees who weren't part of the acquihire were left in limbo, prompting criticism about the "acquihire" trend that enriches founders and investors while leaving rank-and-file employees behind.

Meanwhile, **Cognition AI** (the company behind the Devin AI coding agent) acquired Windsurf's remaining entity and customer base, creating an interesting new player in the AI coding market.

### Why It Matters

The Windsurf deal underscores a new reality in the AI industry: **talent is the bottleneck, and Big Tech is willing to pay staggering sums to acquire it**. Google's $2.4 billion for one startup's team and IP, Meta's $14.3 billion investment in Scale AI to hire CEO Alexandr Wang, and the ongoing bidding wars for top researchers all point to a market where the people who build frontier AI systems are valued almost as much as the systems themselves. The acquihire-plus-licensing structure is also becoming the template for avoiding antitrust scrutiny — expect to see more deals structured this way.

## 🏗️ SoftBank Commits $87.3 Billion to French AI Infrastructure

SoftBank CEO Masayoshi Son announced a staggering **$87.3 billion (€75 billion) investment** in AI data center infrastructure in France, targeting the development of **5 gigawatts of computing capacity** — what would be Europe's largest data center project.

### The Details

- **€49 billion** ($52 billion) committed to data center construction over five years, with the remaining funds allocated to related infrastructure and ecosystem development
- **5 GW target capacity**, making it the largest planned data center project in Europe
- SoftBank's shares have risen over **70% in 2026**, and the group recently overtook Toyota as Japan's most valuable company
- The announcement came after Son met with French President Emmanuel Macron at the **Choose France** summit, which attracted $108 billion in total foreign investment pledges

### The Context

This is SoftBank's largest AI infrastructure investment in Europe to date. It's also part of a broader global push: SoftBank is a major partner in the **Stargate** project with OpenAI and other partners to build hyperscale AI compute capacity in the United States. Son framed the France investment as essential: *"AI is entering a new era, and the countries that build the infrastructure for this transformation will shape the future of technology, industry, and society."*

### Why It Matters

Europe has been playing catch-up in the AI infrastructure race, lagging behind the US and China in GPU capacity. SoftBank's bet on France could change that calculus dramatically. But the announcement also comes as **the EU's own €20 billion AI gigafactory plan is reportedly floundering** due to delays and funding issues, according to Bloomberg. The contrast between SoftBank's private-sector speed and the EU's bureaucratic hurdles is a stark reminder that money alone doesn't build data centers — execution does.

## 🧠 MiniMax M3: An Open-Weight Model Claiming Frontier Coding Performance

Shanghai-based startup **MiniMax** launched **M3** on June 1, positioning it as the first open-weight model to combine frontier-level coding performance, a **one-million-token context window**, and native multimodal capabilities in a single model.

### The Claims

- **59% on SWE-Bench Pro**: MiniMax claims M3 scores 59% on the industry-standard software engineering benchmark, putting it ahead of GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro, though just behind Claude Opus 4.7
- **1 million token context window**: Enabled by a new "MiniMax Sparse Attention" mechanism that the company says stretches context without letting compute costs spiral
- **Native multimodality**: Image, video, and desktop computer operation built into a single model
- **MiniMax Code**: A companion coding agent (available at code.minimax.io) similar to Claude Code or GitHub Copilot

### The Caveats

The AI community has flagged several important caveats:

- **All benchmarks are vendor-reported**, run on MiniMax's own infrastructure — no independent verification yet
- **Open weights have not been released** despite the "open-weight" branding. The company says weights will ship "in approximately 10 days" but they're not available as of the launch
- **China's 2017 National Intelligence Law** requires Chinese companies to "support, assist, and cooperate" with government intelligence work — meaning any data processed through MiniMax's API could theoretically be subject to Chinese government access

### Why It Matters

If MiniMax's benchmarks hold up to independent verification and the weights actually ship, M3 would represent a significant milestone for open-weight AI. A model that genuinely competes with frontier proprietary models on coding tasks — with a 1M context window and multimodal capabilities — would be a game-changer for developers who want capable AI without vendor lock-in. But the gap between MiniMax's claims and the current reality (unreleased weights, unverified benchmarks, data sovereignty concerns) means the jury is still very much out.
