# AI News Roundup: June 26, 2026

The AI industry is getting pulled in two directions at once. On one hand, the US government is loosening restrictions on Anthropic's most powerful model, signaling that American AI companies should be free to compete — but only domestically. On the other hand, OpenAI is losing its IPO momentum, poaching Apple's top hardware executive in a move that suggests it's thinking bigger than Wall Street's timeline. Anthropic and OpenAI are spending millions to shape Washington policy directly, Meta is replacing humans with algorithms at scale, and someone is smuggling AI chips through Kuala Lumpur. The thread connecting all of it: control. Who controls the models, who controls the hardware, who controls the policy — and who gets left out.

## US Loosens Restrictions on Anthropic's Mythos Model, Clearing It for "Trusted" Organizations

**The Trump administration has loosened restrictions on Anthropic's Mythos AI model, allowing it to be released to select "trusted" US organizations**, in a significant policy shift from just weeks ago when the model was fully locked down.

### What's happening

- The US government had previously barred Mythos from being accessed by foreign nationals and restricted its deployment entirely, following concerns about the model's capabilities falling into adversarial hands
- Reuters reports that Mythos will now be available to "trusted" US organizations — a vague but important category that could include government agencies, major corporations, and research institutions
- According to Semafor, the US has released Mythos to "some US companies," while 9to5Mac reports that Anthropic is cleared to release Mythos 5 to "over 100 US institutions"
- WIRED and NBC News both confirmed the green light for a "limited re-release" of Mythos
- The NYT framed it as the US "loosening restrictions," while the Washington Post noted the government "will decide who gets to use the latest American AI technology"

### Why it matters

Two weeks ago, Anthropic's Mythos was under full lockdown — a frontier model deemed too dangerous for foreign access. Now the US government is selectively opening the door, and the implications are enormous. This isn't just about Anthropic; it's about the US government asserting itself as the gatekeeper of frontier AI capabilities. By deciding which organizations are "trusted," the government is creating a two-tier system: American allies and companies get access to the most powerful AI; everyone else doesn't. That's a geopolitical weapon disguised as a regulatory decision.

For Anthropic, this is a mixed blessing. Getting Mythos back into the market restores a revenue stream and competitive position against OpenAI and Google. But the strings attached — government oversight, restricted deployment, and the precedent of regulatory approval as a prerequisite for product launches — fundamentally change the relationship between AI companies and the state. The US government isn't just regulating AI anymore. It's deciding who gets to use it.

## OpenAI Poaches Apple's Vision Pro and Smart Glasses Chief

**OpenAI has hired Apple's top executive overseeing Vision Pro and smart glasses**, in a move that signals OpenAI's ambitions extend well beyond software and into hardware.

### What's happening

- Bloomberg first reported the hire, confirming that the executive leading Apple's mixed reality and smart glasses efforts is departing for OpenAI
- The Information and 9to5Mac both confirmed the move, with 9to5Mac noting this is a significant loss for Apple's Vision Products Group
- The hire comes as OpenAI builds out its hardware capabilities, having already announced its custom Jalapeño inference chip with Broadcom
- Apple has been investing heavily in spatial computing and AI-powered wearables, making this executive's expertise particularly valuable

### Why it matters

OpenAI hiring Apple's Vision Pro chief is one of those moves that makes you rethink what a company actually is. OpenAI started as a research lab, became an API company, then a consumer product company — and now it's building chips and hiring hardware executives. The trajectory is clear: OpenAI wants to own the entire stack, from silicon to software to the device you hold in your hand.

For Apple, this is a gut punch. The Vision Pro is Apple's most ambitious hardware bet in years, and losing the executive responsible for it — and for the smart glasses that are supposed to be the Vision Pro's mass-market successor — suggests internal turmoil or at least a lack of confidence in the product line's trajectory. For OpenAI, this hire is about more than talent. It's about building the hardware layer for AI agents that can see, navigate, and interact with the physical world. If your AI can't see what you see, it can't help you in the ways that matter most.

## OpenAI Leans Toward Delaying IPO to 2027, Dragging Down Oracle and AI Stocks

**OpenAI is reportedly leaning toward holding off on its IPO until next year**, according to the New York Times, sending ripples through the stock market and hitting companies with significant OpenAI exposure.

### What's happening

- The NYT reported that OpenAI is "leaning toward holding up its I.P.O. until next year," a shift from earlier expectations of a 2026 public debut
- Barron's and Investor's Business Daily both reported that Oracle stock took a hit on the news, given Oracle's significant cloud computing relationship with OpenAI
- Seeking Alpha noted that AI memory and chip stocks also dipped globally on the report, with the market reading a delayed IPO as a signal of uncertainty
- The delay comes amid broader market volatility and questions about AI company valuations

### Why it matters

When OpenAI sneezes, the market catches a cold. Oracle stock dropping on IPO delay rumors tells you everything about how much the AI ecosystem has invested — literally — in OpenAI's success. Oracle has bet heavily on being OpenAI's cloud infrastructure partner, and a delayed IPO means a delayed liquidity event for everyone in the chain.

But the deeper question is: why the delay? OpenAI has raised over $30 billion and is reportedly generating billions in revenue. An IPO delay could signal anything from market timing concerns to internal disagreements about valuation to a strategic decision that going public would constrain the company's ability to make aggressive long-term bets. Given that OpenAI is simultaneously building custom chips, hiring Apple's hardware chief, and fighting regulatory battles, maybe the message is that they have too many irons in the fire to satisfy quarterly-earnings-hungry public investors just yet.

## Anthropic and OpenAI Waged a $27 Million Proxy War in a Manhattan Congressional Race

**Anthropic and OpenAI collectively spent $27 million on a proxy war in a Manhattan congressional race**, according to Fortune — and the winner told both companies to get lost.

### What's happening

- Fortune reported that Anthropic and OpenAI poured massive sums into opposing sides of a Democratic primary in Manhattan, each backing candidates aligned with their respective views on AI regulation
- The Verge described the result as ending "in a draw," with neither company's preferred candidate emerging as a clear victor
- NPR noted that AI and tech companies are increasingly trying to influence midterm elections, with this race being the most expensive and visible example
- The winning candidate reportedly distanced themselves from both AI companies, suggesting that the spending may have backfired

### Why it matters

$27 million on a single congressional primary is staggering — and it reveals how desperately both Anthropic and OpenAI want to shape AI regulation in their favor. Anthropic has positioned itself as the "responsible AI" company, favoring strong safety regulations that would make it harder for competitors to deploy powerful models without oversight. OpenAI has taken a more permissive stance, arguing for innovation-friendly regulation that doesn't slow down American AI development.

But here's the twist: the candidate who won rejected both of them. That's a warning sign for the entire AI industry. You can spend millions on political influence, but voters don't like being told what to think by tech billionaires. If AI regulation is going to be shaped by democratic processes rather than corporate lobbying, both companies need a new playbook.

## Meta Plans to Replace 90% of Content Review Staff with AI

**Meta has announced plans to replace approximately 90% of its content review staff with AI systems**, marking one of the most aggressive corporate AI displacement moves to date.

### What's happening

- Social Media Today and the Financial Times both reported on Meta's plan, which would see the vast majority of human content moderators replaced by automated AI systems
- The move comes after years of criticism over Meta's human content moderation — from poor working conditions for moderators to inconsistent enforcement of community standards
- Meta has been investing heavily in AI-powered content analysis, including Llama-based models capable of understanding context, imagery, and multilingual content
- Economy.ac framed the move as part of a broader "AI efficiency backlash," noting that companies from Meta to GM are replacing workers with AI systems

### Why it matters

This is the most concrete example yet of AI displacing a large, existing workforce at a major tech company. Meta's content moderation team numbers in the tens of thousands globally, and replacing 90% of them would mean eliminating one of the largest human workforces in social media. Meta will argue that AI is faster, more consistent, and doesn't suffer from the psychological trauma that human moderators have reported. Critics will argue that AI systems are notoriously bad at understanding context, satire, and cultural nuance — the exact things that make content moderation hard.

The reality is probably somewhere in between. AI will handle the easy cases — obvious spam, clear policy violations — but the edge cases that define free speech boundaries will still need human judgment. Whether Meta keeps enough humans in the loop for those cases remains to be seen. What's certain is that this is the template: if Meta can do it, every other platform will try.

## Malaysia Seizes $13 Million in Smuggled AI Chips at Kuala Lumpur Airport

**Malaysian customs has seized AI chips worth $13 million at Kuala Lumpur International Airport**, exposing the shadowy trade in restricted AI hardware.

### What's happening

- Reuters first reported the seizure, with the chips intercepted at Kuala Lumpur airport in what appears to be a smuggling operation
- NDTV and Khaleej Times both covered the story, confirming the $13 million valuation
- The seizure comes amid increasingly tight US export controls on advanced AI chips, particularly Nvidia GPUs, to China and other restricted destinations
- Malaysia has become a known transshipment point for chips heading to restricted markets, with smugglers exploiting the country's busy air cargo hubs

### Why it matters

$13 million in smuggled AI chips at one airport is a window into a much larger problem. As the US tightens export controls on advanced semiconductors — particularly Nvidia's most powerful GPUs — a black market has emerged to bypass those restrictions. Malaysia has been identified as a key node in this network, with chips arriving legally and then being rerouted to China and other restricted destinations. Every seizure like this raises the question: how much is getting through? The US export control regime is only as strong as its weakest link, and if smugglers can move $13 million worth of chips through a single airport, the regime has some serious weak links.

## Google Loses 5 Top AI Researchers in 7 Days as Gemini Falls Behind

**Five senior AI researchers have departed Google in the span of a single week**, according to multiple reports, as concerns mount about Gemini's competitive position.

### What's happening

- CNBC and Yahoo Finance both reported that five high-profile AI researchers left Google in just seven days, an unusually concentrated brain drain
- The departures come amid growing concerns about Gemini falling behind OpenAI and Anthropic in benchmark performance and developer mindshare
- MarketWatch framed the exodus as highlighting "how human brains may be the scarcest AI resource of all"
- Alphabet stock dropped following the reports, with investors questioning whether Google can maintain its AI competitiveness

### Why it matters

In AI, talent is the ultimate moat. Models can be replicated, compute can be bought, and data can be scraped — but the researchers who can push the frontier forward are finite and increasingly mobile. Google losing five top researchers in a week suggests something is broken internally, whether it's compensation, culture, or strategic direction. When the people building your models start leaving for competitors, the models themselves won't be far behind. Google has the resources to compete, but resources only matter if the people who know how to use them stick around.

## The Big Picture

June 26, 2026 was a day about who controls what. The US government decided it controls who gets to use Anthropic's most powerful model. OpenAI decided it controls its own timeline, even if that means delaying its IPO and spooking the market. Anthropic and OpenAI tried to control Congress — and got rejected. Meta decided algorithms control content better than humans. Malaysian customs reminded us that even chips have a black market. And Google's brain drain reminded us that the most important AI resource can't be manufactured or mined — it walks out the door.

The AI industry is entering its regulatory era, whether it likes it or not. Governments are asserting control over model deployment. Companies are spending millions to control policy. And the underlying technologies — chips, supply chains, talent — are being treated as strategic assets worthy of smuggling, hoarding, and fighting over. The next phase of AI isn't going to be won by whoever builds the smartest model. It's going to be won by whoever controls the infrastructure, the talent, and the regulations that shape how AI gets built and deployed. The model wars are over. The control wars have begun.
