# AI News Roundup: June 17, 2026

The Fable 5 fallout went global today. At the G7 Summit, world leaders told the US — to its face — that the ability to shut off AI access at will is a dealbreaker for allied nations. Meanwhile, Anthropic burnished its environmental credentials by joining a carbon removal coalition, China's Z.ai released an open-weights model that beats proprietary giants, Google put Gemini into a smart speaker, and a Pew study confirmed what many suspected: Americans are deeply skeptical of AI. Here's the full roundup.

## G7 Leaders to US: We Want Your AI, But Not With a Kill Switch

The biggest story of the day came from the G7 Summit, where **French President Emmanuel Macron and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi** both voiced concerns that the US could cut off their countries' access to top American AI models at any time.

### What happened

- At a G7 lunch attended by Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman, and President Trump, **Macron warned that if the US "from one day to the next can turn off the switch,"** it could harm European economies and damage the AI firms themselves
- **PM Modi** echoed the concern, saying democratic nations must have unfettered access to top AI models to protect critical infrastructure
- The context is clear: the Trump administration's decision to block Anthropic from exporting Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on national security grounds has spooked US allies worldwide

### The Cohere angle

Cohere CEO **Aidan Gomez** seized the moment: "The recent restriction on access to Anthropic's models confirms what we at Cohere have known all along: that companies and democratic nations remaining dependent on a small handful of big tech companies is dangerous to resilience."

This is a direct pitch for sovereign AI infrastructure. Cohere, which focuses on enterprise deployments with data locality guarantees, stands to benefit enormously from the trust crisis the Fable 5 ban has created.

### Why it matters

The US is discovering that AI dominance isn't just about having the best models — it's about whether allies trust that access won't be revoked. Every time Washington pulls the kill switch, it pushes another country toward open-source alternatives and domestic AI investment. France's move to dump Palantir (yesterday's story) is just the beginning.

## Anthropic Becomes First AI Startup to Join Frontier Carbon Removal Coalition

While Anthropic fights the White House over export controls, it's also making moves on climate. The company announced it has **joined the Frontier carbon removal coalition**, contributing to a new **$915 million funding tranche** that nearly doubles Frontier's total pledges to $1.8 billion.

### Why this matters

- Anthropic is the **first pure AI company** to join Frontier, which was founded by Stripe, Google, and Shopify
- AI companies have been on an energy buying spree, and not always with clean sources. Anthropic itself has favored an "all of the above" energy approach — which usually means significant fossil fuel consumption
- This is Anthropic's **first climate-related deal**, and the company has yet to publish a sustainability report
- The move signals that Anthropic is serious about addressing its growing environmental footprint — or at least serious about being seen addressing it

### The skeptical read

Carbon removal credits let companies continue emitting pollution while claiming they're working toward net zero. Frontier vets the projects, but the fundamental tension remains: AI companies are consuming more energy than ever, and offsets don't reduce emissions — they just cancel them out on paper.

## Z.ai's GLM-5.2 Becomes the Leading Open-Weights Model

China's Z.ai (formerly Zhipu AI) released **GLM-5.2**, and it's a landmark moment for open-source AI. The model **scores 51 on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index v4.1**, making it the top open-weights model and placing it on the Pareto frontier of intelligence vs. cost.

### Key benchmarks

- **Outperforms MiniMax-M3 (44) and DeepSeek V4 Pro (44)** on the Intelligence Index
- **Scores 1524 on GDPval-AA v2**, competitive with proprietary models including GPT-5.5
- **Scientific reasoning gains**: +16 points on CritPt, +12 points on HLE
- **Context window expanded to 1M tokens** (up from 200K on GLM-5.1)
- **Same architecture as GLM-5.1**: 744B total / 40B active parameters
- **MIT licensed** — fully open
- **Priced at $1.4/$0.26/$4.4 per 1M input/cache/output tokens**

### The bigger picture

This is the third consecutive week where Chinese open-weights models have matched or beaten proprietary alternatives. The US government's export controls on frontier models like Fable 5 aren't just affecting Anthropic — they're **accelerating the shift toward open-source models that no government can shut down**. The Wall Street Journal's recent op-ed got it right: "China's Z.ai and America's self-defeating AI strategy."

GLM-5.2 isn't just good for an open model. It's good, period. And at roughly one-sixth the cost of GPT-5.5, it's the kind of price-performance ratio that reshapes enterprise procurement decisions.

## Only 16% of Americans Think AI Will Have a Positive Impact on Society

A new **Pew Research study** dropped a sobering data point: **just 16% of Americans believe AI will have a positive impact on society** over the next 20 years. Roughly 40% expect a negative impact, and a vast majority (67%) don't believe the government will meaningfully regulate AI.

### The details

- **59% of Americans don't trust companies** to develop AI safely
- **Nearly two-thirds** think AI is developing too quickly
- **Young people are the most skeptical**: only 14% of those under 30 see a positive future for AI
- **ChatGPT dominates usage**: 44% of US adults now use it, more than double since 2023
- Gemini (24%), Copilot (17%), and Meta AI (14%) trail significantly
- **Men use AI more and are more enthusiastic**; women are more skeptical

### Why it matters

There's a growing disconnect between the AI industry's hype and public sentiment. Companies are raising at trillion-dollar valuations, governments are scrambling for sovereignty, and yet the people who actually use these tools are increasingly doubtful. The 16% figure should be a warning sign for every AI company preparing an IPO roadshow.

## Google Bets on Gemini to Reinvent the Smart Home Speaker

Google launched the **$99.99 Google Home Speaker**, its first standalone smart speaker since the Nest Audio in 2020, and it's built entirely around Gemini.

### What's new

- **Natural language processing** that actually works: "Turn off all the lights except my bedside lamp" works without precise phrasing
- **Mid-sentence corrections**: "Turn off the coffee maker… I mean, turn it on!" — Gemini understands
- **10 new conversational voices** that can discuss topics beyond smart home commands
- **Continued Conversation** keeps the mic on briefly so follow-ups don't need "OK, Google"
- Ships in **Jade and Berry colors** (US only) plus Hazel and Porcelain globally

### The strategic play

This isn't just a speaker — it's Google's bet that **Gemini is the interface layer** for the home. Apple is still trying to make Siri happen (with Gemini under the hood), Amazon's Alexa is ad-supported, and Google is going all-in on conversational AI that actually works. The $99 price point is aggressive — Google is clearly willing to lose money on hardware to get Gemini into living rooms.

## Odyssey Raises $310M for World Models at $1.45B Valuation

**Odyssey**, a world model AI startup founded by self-driving vehicle pioneers Oliver Cameron and Jeff Hawke, raised a **$310 million Series B at a $1.45 billion valuation** led by Natural Capital, with Amazon, AMD Ventures, and GV participating.

### What are world models?

World models go beyond text — they **simulate the physical world with accurate physics**, generating rich interactive video from text prompts. Odyssey's founders previously built autonomous vehicle companies (Cameron's Voyage was acquired by GM's Cruise; Hawke worked at Wayve).

### Key details

- **AWS is now Odyssey's preferred cloud provider**, and the startup will optimize its models for AWS's Trainium chips (a competitor to Nvidia)
- Impressive angel investors: **Jeff Dean, Elad Gil, Garry Tan, Guillermo Rauch, and Cruise founder Kyle Vogt**
- The startup has raised **$337 million to date**
- Use cases span video game creation, robotics, and interactive video

World models are emerging as the next frontier after LLMs — and Amazon's investment signals the cloud giant wants to own this layer the way it owns the cloud infrastructure layer.

## XDOF Emerges From Stealth With $70M for Robot Training Data

The robotics data problem is getting its own infrastructure company. **XDOF** (pronounced "ecks-doff") emerged from stealth with **$70 million in funding** from Thrive Capital, Spark Capital, a16z, Lux, and WndrCo.

### The problem they're solving

OpenAI just relaunched its robotics program, and every frontier lab is racing toward physical AI. But unlike LLMs (trained on freely available text), **robots need physical interaction data that barely exists**. YouTube videos and gig-worker footage are low-fidelity and hard to reconcile with the real world.

### What XDOF does

- Builds **data pipelines, collection tools, and annotation systems** for robotics training
- Already working with **20 customers including several frontier AI labs**
- Founded by Philipp Wu, a UC Berkeley PhD who hit this exact problem in his research
- The company has ~60 employees

This is a classic "picks and shovels" play. If physical AI is the gold rush, XDOF is selling the data infrastructure that makes it possible.

## Pramaana Labs Raises $27M Seed for Formal Verification in AI

**Pramaana Labs** raised a **$27 million seed round led by Khosla Ventures**, with participation from Accel, BoldCap, Nexus Venture Partners, and Premji Invest.

### What they're building

- **Formal verification for AI**: using the LEAN programming language (the same one used to verify mathematical proofs) to create a deterministic layer on top of LLMs
- Targets **high-stakes verticals** where errors are costly: law, drug discovery, and tax preparation
- Working with **former IRS commissioner Danny Werfel** on tax law formalization
- The approach: LLM for flexibility, LEAN-style formal verification for reliability

This is the kind of infrastructure that enterprise AI needs. If agents are going to file your taxes or review your contracts, they need to be right — not just confident. Pramaana is building the verification layer that makes that possible.

## US Holds Off Blacklisting DeepSeek, but 100+ Firms Deemed Security Risks

Reuters reports that the **US government has held off on adding China's DeepSeek to its blacklist**, even as more than 100 other firms have been deemed security risks. The decision reflects the ongoing tension between restricting Chinese AI capabilities and acknowledging that open-source models can't easily be contained.

This comes the same week GLM-5.2 topped open-weights benchmarks. The US can restrict exports of proprietary models like Fable 5, but it can't put the open-source genie back in the bottle.

## Android 17 Launches With Gemini Deep Integration

Google released the **final version of Android 17** alongside Wear OS 7, and it's the most Gemini-heavy Android release yet:

- **Gemini Omni** lets you edit videos in conversation
- **Lyria 3** creates music tracks from text prompts and/or images in the Gemini app
- **AudioLM** brings speech-to-speech translation to Pixel 10a devices
- A new **"bubble bar"** UI helps organize recent apps at the bottom of the screen

Android 17 is Google's clearest statement yet: the operating system is becoming a delivery vehicle for Gemini. While Apple scrambles to make Siri competent, Google is baking music generation, video editing, and real-time translation directly into the OS.
