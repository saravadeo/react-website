## ⚖️ Musk v. Altman — Jury Deliberations Begin

Closing arguments wrapped last week in the landmark **Elon Musk v. OpenAI** trial in Oakland, and the case is now in the jury's hands. The verdict could reshape how AI companies are structured — and who controls them.

### What the Jury Must Decide

1. **Timeliness** — Did Musk file the lawsuit in time, or is it barred by the statute of limitations?
2. **Charitable trust** — If timely, did OpenAI's original nonprofit structure constitute a "charitable trust" that OpenAI and its executives breached?
3. **Microsoft's role** — Did Microsoft, as co-defendant, aid and abet that breach?

### Key Testimony Highlights

- **Sam Altman** testified that Musk knew about plans to develop OpenAI into a for-profit enterprise when he invested, and revealed that **Musk petitioned for a majority stake** — up to 90% — in the company. Altman told the court: "I don't think Mr. Musk understood how to run a good research lab."
- **Elon Musk** portrayed Altman as someone who "could not be trusted" with the development of transformative technology, calling the for-profit conversion equivalent to "looting a charity."
- **Greg Brockman** testified alongside Altman, defending the restructuring as necessary to secure the compute funding needed to compete.

### Why It Matters

If the jury finds a charitable trust existed and was breached, it could force OpenAI to restructure — potentially unwinding the for-profit PBC conversion that underpins its $852B valuation. Microsoft, as co-defendant, faces liability for aiding and abetting. A verdict in Musk's favor would send shockwaves through every AI startup's cap table.

CNN's roundup framed the trial as one of four major AI stories shaping the current landscape, alongside Bumble's AI dating concierge, a lawsuit claiming ChatGPT encouraged an FSU shooter, and expert warnings against using AI for financial advice.

## 🚀 Cerebras IPO — Biggest Tech Listing Since Snowflake

Cerebras Systems (NASDAQ: **CBRS**) debuted Thursday at **$350 per share** — nearly double its **$185 IPO price** — making it the biggest US tech IPO since Snowflake in 2020. Shares closed the first day at **$311.07, up 68%**, valuing the AI chipmaker at over **$100 billion**.

### By the Numbers

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| IPO price | $185/share |
| Opening price | $350/share |
| First-day close | $311.07 (+68%) |
| Market cap at close | ~$100B+ |
| IPO raise | $5.5B |

### Reality Check

The euphoria didn't last long. By Friday, shares **dropped ~10%** from the first-day close as analysts flagged concerns about profitability and the gap between Cerebras' revenue and its towering valuation. The Motley Fool compared Cerebras' trajectory to Snowflake — which also doubled on its debut in 2020 but left first-day buyers underwater for years.

Wedbush analyst Dan Ives called Cerebras "the appetizer," noting that **future IPOs from SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic** could generate even larger market enthusiasm.

### The Bigger Picture

Cerebras makes the world's largest AI chip — nearly a foot long across its diagonal — designed to compete with NVIDIA's GPU dominance. The blockbuster debut signals that investor appetite for AI infrastructure plays remains insatiable, even as questions about sustainable revenue multiply.

## 💼 LinkedIn Cuts 900 Jobs Despite Record Revenue

Microsoft-owned LinkedIn is laying off **~5% of its global workforce** — roughly **875 to 900 employees** — even as the company posted **12% year-on-year revenue growth** and crossed **$5 billion in quarterly revenue** for the first time.

### The Paradox

A company with double-digit revenue growth and record quarterly earnings is still showing people the door. CEO Ryan Roslansky cited "shifts in customer behavior and slower revenue growth" and a move to "a flatter organizational structure." LinkedIn is also shutting its office in Graz, Austria.

### AI as the Subtext

The layoffs are part of a broader pattern across Big Tech. LinkedIn's cuts push the **2026 tech layoff total past 100,000 workers**, according to tracking by The Next Web. Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet, Oracle, and IBM have all cut staff this year — even as the same companies are guiding **$725 billion in combined AI capital spending** for 2026.

The irony writes itself: the platform that promises to help professionals find jobs is now pushing its own employees to post "Open to Work" banners. As one analyst noted: AI accounted for roughly **26% of US job cuts in April 2026** alone.

### The 100K Club

With LinkedIn's cuts, the list of Big Tech companies that have laid off workers in 2026 now includes Meta (~8,000), Amazon (~16,000), Snap (~1,000), Atlassian (~1,600), HP, and others. The pattern is clear: AI is not just a product opportunity — it's becoming a restructuring justification.

## ⏱️ Microsoft's Suleyman: 18 Months to White-Collar Automation

Microsoft AI CEO **Mustafa Suleyman** made headlines this week with a stark prediction: **all white-collar work could be automated within 18 months**.

Speaking to Fortune, Suleyman argued that AI agents are approaching the capability threshold where they can handle the full spectrum of knowledge work — from research and analysis to drafting, decision-making, and execution. The timeline is far more aggressive than most industry forecasts, which typically place widespread white-collar automation in the 3-5 year range.

### Context

Suleyman's prediction comes as Microsoft is aggressively shipping agentic AI products across its Copilot platform and Azure AI Foundry. Microsoft's Build 2026 conference is weeks away, where further agentic tooling announcements are expected.

Whether Suleyman's 18-month timeline is prescient or premature, it reflects the intensity of the current AI deployment race — and raises urgent questions about workforce readiness, retraining, and the social safety nets needed if the prediction proves even half-right.

## 🕊️ Pope Leo XIV Drafts First Papal Encyclical on AI

Pope Leo XIV is preparing the **first-ever papal encyclical on artificial intelligence**, according to OSV News and the Associated Press. The document — the highest form of papal teaching — will address AI's impact on human dignity, labor, truth, and the common good.

### Why a Pope Named Leo?

The choice of name is deliberate. Pope Leo XIII wrote *Rerum Novarum* in 1891 — the foundational encyclical on workers' rights during the Industrial Revolution. Pope Leo XIV has explicitly framed AI as the defining technological challenge of our era, equivalent to the industrial upheaval his namesake addressed.

### What to Expect

The encyclical is expected to cover:
- **Human dignity in the age of AI** — the moral status of AI-generated content and decisions
- **Labor displacement** — the Church's position on AI-driven job losses
- **Truth and misinformation** — deepfakes, AI-generated propaganda, and epistemic crisis
- **The common good** — whether AI development serves humanity broadly or concentrates power

The Vatican has been engaging with tech leaders on AI ethics since 2024. This encyclical would mark the most significant institutional moral framework applied to AI by any major religious authority.

## 💕 Bumble Abandons the Swipe for AI Dating Concierge

Dating app Bumble is **killing its signature swipe interface** in favor of an **AI "dating concierge"** that handles matchmaking through conversation. The shift is one of the most radical UX overhauls in consumer tech — replacing the gesture that defined mobile dating with an AI-mediated experience.

The concierge chats with users to understand preferences, then interacts with potential matches' AI concierges to find compatibility — essentially replacing the swipe with an AI-to-AI negotiation layer.

### What It Signals

Bumble's move is a bet that AI-native UX patterns will replace the interaction paradigms that defined the smartphone era. If dating — one of the most personal, high-stakes consumer decisions — can be delegated to AI, few app categories are safe from AI-first redesigns.
