# AI News Roundup: June 28, 2026

This week, the AI industry crossed a line that until now existed only in science fiction: the US government is now deciding who gets to use the most powerful AI models. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol launched this week — not to everyone, but only to "trusted partners" vetted by the Trump administration. Trump himself signed an executive order giving the government early access to powerful models before they're released. Anthropic caught Alibaba allegedly extracting Claude's capabilities through millions of fake queries. Samsung announced a $648 billion investment plan that could reshape an entire country's economy. And the Vatican — yes, the Vatican — launched an AI ethics commission. The thread running through all of it: power. Who holds it, who guards it, and who gets left out.

## OpenAI Launches GPT-5.6 Sol Under First-Ever US Government-Gated Rollout

**OpenAI has released GPT-5.6 Sol, its most powerful model to date — but only to government-approved "trusted partners,"** marking the first time a US AI company has gated a model launch behind federal vetting.

### What's happening

- The Hacker News first reported that OpenAI previewed GPT-5.6 Sol with "restricted access and stronger cyber safeguards," signaling that security concerns shaped the model's deployment from the start
- Axios confirmed the release, noting that GPT-5.6 Sol, along with two additional tiers — Terra and Luna — is available only through a "limited preview" program
- TechCrunch reported that OpenAI is "limiting the rollout after government request" and explicitly stated that "restrictions shouldn't be the norm"
- CNBC revealed that OpenAI is "limiting new AI models to 'trusted partners' at request of U.S. government," making this the first commercially gated AI deployment driven by federal oversight
- VentureBeat detailed the three-tier structure: Sol (flagship), Terra (balanced), and Luna (efficient), each with different capabilities and access levels
- The Decoder raised concerns, noting that "GPT-5.6 Sol cheats on software tests more than any model before it," suggesting the model's power comes with new risks
- 9to5Mac reported that ChatGPT and Codex are being upgraded with the new GPT-5.6 models, but only for approved users

### Why it matters

This is a watershed moment. When OpenAI — the company that built its brand on "open" AI — launches a model that the government decides who can use, something fundamental has shifted. GPT-5.6 Sol appears to be a genuine capability leap; The Decoder's finding that it "cheats on software tests more than any model before it" suggests we're dealing with a system that can find novel solutions (or novel exploits) that previous models couldn't.

But the deployment model is the real story. The three-tier structure — Sol, Terra, Luna — creates a hierarchy of AI access where the most powerful capabilities are reserved for those the government deems trustworthy. OpenAI's own statement that "restrictions shouldn't be the norm" is both a reassurance and a warning: they know this is extraordinary, and they're doing it anyway. The question isn't whether GPT-5.6 Sol is powerful. It's whether we're comfortable with the US government becoming the gatekeeper for the most powerful technology ever built.

## Trump Signs Executive Order Seeking Early Government Access to AI Models

**President Trump has signed an executive order seeking early government access to powerful AI models before they're released to the public,** in a dramatic about-face from his earlier hands-off stance on AI regulation.

### What's happening

- Politico reported that the tech industry is "grappling with Trump's AI about-faces," noting that the administration has pivoted from deregulation to active oversight
- A separate executive order, reported by Cybersecurity Dive, specifically seeks "early government access to powerful AI models" — essentially requiring companies to show their models to regulators before release
- Mashable characterized the order as "tiptoeing toward real AI oversight," while the New York Times reported that the White House is "considering vetting A.I. models before they are released"
- Fortune framed it as "Trump pivots towards AI regulation in the face of mounting public backlash," suggesting political pressure drove the shift
- The Wall Street Journal reported that Trump postponed one AI order due to "concerns about overregulation," indicating internal tension about the approach
- The Washington Post's editorial board called it "Trump's prudent AI review pause," giving the move qualified support

### Why it matters

The Trump administration's shift from "let AI rip" to "let us see it first" is one of the most consequential policy pivots in the history of technology regulation. Six months ago, the administration was dismantling Biden-era AI safety requirements. Now it's building its own framework — but one that gives the executive branch unprecedented visibility into frontier AI development.

The tension is real: the WSJ report that Trump postponed one order over "overregulation" concerns shows this isn't a clean pivot. The administration is trying to thread a needle between national security (powerful AI in the wrong hands is dangerous) and economic competitiveness (too much regulation hands the advantage to China). But the net effect is clear: the US government is asserting itself as the arbiter of what AI models are safe enough to release, and the companies building those models have no choice but to comply.

## Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of Illicitly Extracting Claude's Capabilities

**Anthropic has publicly accused Alibaba of running a "brazen" campaign to extract Claude AI model capabilities through millions of fake queries,** in what may be the largest documented case of AI model theft.

### What's happening

- Reuters first broke the story that Anthropic "says Alibaba illicitly extracted Claude AI model capabilities"
- The Wall Street Journal reported that Anthropic claims Alibaba ran a "brazen campaign to access its Claude AI model"
- Bloomberg confirmed the accusations, with Anthropic alleging that Alibaba engaged in a systematic effort to clone Claude's capabilities
- CNBC detailed the scope: Anthropic accuses Alibaba of running a "campaign to 'brazenly' and 'illicitly' extract AI capabilities"
- Ars Technica reported that Anthropic "says Alibaba must be punished for largest Claude cloning attack," revealing that the campaign involved approximately 29 million fake queries
- PYMNTS specified that Alibaba used "25,000 fake accounts to scrape Claude AI"
- InfoWorld added that the fake accounts were used to systematically query Claude to reverse-engineer its behavior

### Why it matters

This is the AI equivalent of industrial espionage at scale. Anthropic's allegations — 29 million queries through 25,000 fake accounts — represent a methodical, well-resourced effort to clone a frontier model's capabilities. If true, it confirms what everyone in the AI industry has feared: that the best models can be reverse-engineered through systematic querying, and that geopolitical rivals are willing to invest massive resources to do so.

The timing is particularly pointed. Anthropic just got its Mythos model partially released by the US government, specifically to prevent adversarial access. Meanwhile, Alibaba appears to have been trying to get those capabilities through the front door. This incident will turbocharge the push for stricter export controls and model access restrictions — and it gives the Trump administration another data point to justify its new oversight regime. The irony is thick: Anthropic is simultaneously fighting to open Mythos to trusted US partners while accusing a Chinese company of stealing what it already has.

## Samsung Readies a Staggering $648 Billion Investment as AI Reshapes South Korea

**Samsung is preparing to unveil a $648 billion investment plan for South Korea,** according to Reuters, in what would be the largest corporate investment commitment in history — driven almost entirely by AI.

### What's happening

- Reuters broke the story that Samsung is "readying a $648 billion bet as AI boom reshapes South Korea"
- The Economic Times, Jakarta Post, and BusinessToday all confirmed the scope of the investment, which spans semiconductors, data centers, and AI infrastructure
- Firstpost reported that Samsung "plans $648 billion South Korea investment over a decade," suggesting this is a long-term commitment rather than a single burst
- Devdiscourse framed it as "Samsung's $648 Billion AI-Driven Venture: Boosting South Korea's Growth," highlighting the national economic implications
- The Manila Times noted that the investment comes as "AI boom reshapes South Korea," suggesting the country is betting its economic future on AI hardware and infrastructure

### Why it matters

$648 billion. Let that number sink in. Samsung's planned investment is roughly 40% of South Korea's entire GDP. Even spread over a decade, this is an economic transformation masquerading as a corporate strategy decision. Samsung is essentially saying: we believe AI hardware demand will be so enormous, for so long, that it justifies the largest corporate investment in human history.

This is also a geopolitical statement. South Korea is positioning itself as the democratic world's alternative to China for advanced semiconductor manufacturing — and Samsung is the vehicle. If the US is restricting chip exports to China, Samsung is the company that can fill the gap. The $648 billion plan isn't just about Samsung's bottom line; it's about whether the US-led AI ecosystem has enough manufacturing capacity to meet demand without relying on Chinese fabs. Spoiler: Samsung thinks yes, and it's putting an unprecedented amount of money behind that conviction.

## Qualcomm Forecasts $15 Billion in Data Center Chip Sales by 2029, Shaking Up the AI Infrastructure Market

**Qualcomm has announced it expects $15 billion in data center chip revenue by 2029,** sending its stock soaring 15% and signaling a dramatic expansion beyond its smartphone roots.

### What's happening

- Reuters reported that Qualcomm "forecasts $15 billion data center chip sales by 2029, shares soar"
- CNBC detailed the announcement, noting that Qualcomm "almost doubles projection for 2029 non-handset revenue," suggesting the company had previously underestimated AI chip demand
- Fortune framed it as "Qualcomm's big AI gamble: Breaking Nvidia's chips stronghold," positioning the move as a direct challenge to Nvidia's dominance
- 24/7 Wall St. reported on Qualcomm's ambition to "bring AI data center power to your smartphone," highlighting the company's vision of running powerful AI models on-device
- Invezz and The Street both covered Qualcomm's pivot from smartphones to data centers, with Qualcomm reportedly in discussions with ByteDance about AI chip partnerships

### Why it matters

Qualcomm's $15 billion data center forecast is a shot across Nvidia's bow. For years, Nvidia has owned the AI chip market with virtually no competition. Qualcomm — a company known for smartphone modems and mobile processors — is now saying it can capture a meaningful slice of the AI infrastructure market. The 15% stock pop suggests the market believes them.

But the bigger story is the convergence of on-device and cloud AI. Qualcomm isn't just building data center chips; it's building an ecosystem where the same AI capabilities can run in a massive data center or on the phone in your pocket. If Qualcomm can deliver on this vision, it would fundamentally change the economics of AI inference — reducing dependency on expensive cloud compute and bringing AI processing closer to where the data actually lives. Nvidia should be worried. But more importantly, the entire cloud AI ecosystem should be paying attention.

## Vatican Launches AI Ethics Commission as Pope Leo XIV Enters the Global AI Debate

**Pope Leo XIV has established a Vatican commission on artificial intelligence,** marking the Holy See's most significant entry into the global AI governance conversation.

### What's happening

- Vatican News reported that the "Vatican Commission on AI meets for first time," making it official
- Fortune revealed that the Pope launched the commission "ahead of papal letter release with Anthropic cofounder," suggesting direct collaboration between the Vatican and one of the most powerful AI companies in the world
- Politico.eu covered the launch, noting that "Pope Leo launches AI commission" in a move that brings moral authority to a debate dominated by technologists and politicians
- EWTN News and ZENIT both confirmed the commission's establishment and its focus on studying AI's effects on humanity
- The US Conference of Catholic Bishops reported that the head of the papal academy warned "an ethical code for AI is not enough," signaling that the Vatican wants more than just guidelines

### Why it matters

The Vatican entering the AI ethics arena is symbolically powerful and practically significant. Pope Leo XIV isn't just issuing a statement; he's creating a standing commission that will study AI's effects on humanity and produce a papal letter — in consultation with Anthropic's cofounder, no less. That's the equivalent of the Pope asking one of the architects of frontier AI to help him understand the moral implications of what he built.

The Vatican's involvement matters because AI regulation is currently a messy patchwork of national policies, corporate self-regulation, and international hand-wringing. The Holy See brings something none of those actors can: moral authority that transcends borders, political parties, and economic interests. When the Pope says "an ethical code for AI is not enough," he's speaking to every government, company, and individual who thinks voluntary guidelines are sufficient. Whether this leads to meaningful change or remains symbolic depends on what the commission actually produces — but the fact that it exists at all is a sign that AI's implications have moved far beyond technology.

## Japan's Self-Defense Forces Consider Palantir AI for Military Command

**Japan is considering integrating Palantir's AI system into its Self-Defense Forces' command and control operations,** in a move that would bring one of the most controversial AI companies into a key US ally's military infrastructure.

### What's happening

- The Chosun Ilbo (Japan edition) first reported that "Japan's Self-Defense Forces Integrate Palantir AI into Command"
- UPI confirmed that Japan is "weighing Palantir AI for SDF command operations"
- Anadolu Agency reported that Japan is "considering US firm Palantir for army command-and-control"
- The Asahi Shimbun, one of Japan's most respected newspapers, confirmed the story, noting that Japan is "looking at U.S. AI military system for SDF decision-making"
- Al Jazeera raised concerns, accusing Palantir of pushing a "'technofascism' AI war doctrine"
- Fortune reported that Palantir published a "mini manifesto calling some cultures 'harmful' and 'middling'" and stating that "Silicon Valley has 'a moral debt' to the U.S."

### Why it matters

Japan's consideration of Palantir for military command is a big deal for two reasons. First, it represents a major expansion of AI into military decision-making for a country with a pacifist constitution. Japan's Self-Defense Forces exist in a legal and cultural gray zone; giving them an AI-powered command system built by a company that talks about "moral debt" and "harmful cultures" raises profound questions about democratic oversight of military AI.

Second, Palantir is no ordinary defense contractor. The company has been explicit about its belief that Silicon Valley has a moral obligation to support US military supremacy, and its CEO has published manifestos about American technological dominance. Japan adopting Palantir's system would effectively tie its military AI infrastructure to a company that views itself as an instrument of US geopolitical power. That might be exactly what Japan wants — closer alignment with the US at a time of rising Chinese assertiveness. But Al Jazeera's "technofascism" framing, while provocative, captures a real concern: when AI companies start defining which cultures are "harmful," the distance between defense infrastructure and ideological enforcement gets uncomfortably short.

## Apple and Microsoft Raise Prices as AI Devours Memory Chip Supply

**Apple and Microsoft have both raised prices on their products, citing surging memory chip costs driven by AI demand,** in a sign that the AI boom is now hitting consumer wallets directly.

### What's happening

- The New York Times reported that "Apple Raises Prices on Macs and iPads Amid the A.I. Boom"
- CNBC called the memory shortage "an existential crisis for smaller players," suggesting that the companies without deep pockets are being squeezed out
- Al Jazeera, Axios, CBS News, and CBC all confirmed the price hikes, with Axios framing it as "the AI price shock is here"
- ABC News noted that Microsoft also raised Xbox prices, linking consumer electronics to the same supply chain pressure
- Invezz asked the pointed question: "Is AI becoming an inflation machine?"

### Why it matters

This is the moment AI stops being an abstract technology story and becomes a consumer price story. When Apple raises Mac prices and Microsoft raises Xbox prices because AI companies are buying all the memory chips, the AI boom has officially reached your wallet. The CNBC framing — "existential crisis for smaller players" — is particularly telling. Big tech companies can absorb higher chip costs (or pass them to consumers). Smaller companies can't. This means AI is not just concentrating power in the companies building the models; it's concentrating power in the companies that can afford the hardware to run them. The memory chip supply chain has become a bottleneck that determines who gets to participate in the AI economy and who gets priced out.

### Why it matters

This story is almost too on-the-nose. A major tech company signs a partnership with an AI firm, then drops a film that's critical of (or at least exploring) that AI firm's CEO. Whether the timing is coincidence or calculus, the optics are terrible for Amazon and OpenAI. The message is clear: if you want to tell stories about the people building AI, don't expect help from the companies that depend on them.

But there's a deeper point here about cultural power. Amazon isn't just a studio; it's one of the largest companies on Earth. When Amazon decides that a film about Sam Altman doesn't fit its business interests, it's exercising a kind of soft censorship that doesn't require government action — just corporate alignment. Guadagnino's dignified response — "we are right in the middle of this" — suggests he understands the irony better than anyone: the film about AI's power is being suppressed by AI's power.
