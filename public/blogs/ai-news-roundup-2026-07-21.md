## US AI Safety Chief Resigns After Just 3 Months

**Chris Fall, director of the Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), has resigned after only three months on the job. Arvind Raman has been named acting chief. The timing couldn't be worse — the US AI safety apparatus is now leaderless during the most consequential summer in AI regulation.**

### What happened

- Chris Fall resigned as director of CAISI, the federal government's primary AI safety body housed within the Commerce Department
- He had been in the role for only three months — a tenure so short it raises more questions than it answers
- Arvind Raman has been named acting chief, but the permanent leadership vacuum comes at a critical moment
- The resignation was first reported by Axios and confirmed by multiple outlets including The Hill, Politico, and Reuters

### Why it matters

Three months is not a tenure — it's a layover. The head of the nation's AI safety institute barely had time to learn where the bathrooms are before departing. This comes at the worst possible time:

- **The EU is moving forward with the AI Act**, creating enforceable standards that will shape the global market
- **MIT just published a survey of 272 AI experts** identifying the most urgent risks — and those risks are exactly the ones a functioning safety institute should be addressing
- **A bipartisan survey showed majority support for federal AI safety regulations** — the political will exists, but the institutional capacity is cratering
- **China is weighing export controls on AI models** while the US can't even staff its safety office

The resignation also raises uncomfortable questions about the Trump administration's commitment to AI safety. CAISI was never given the kind of authority or budget that its predecessor NIST AI Safety Institute had. When the director leaves after 90 days, it suggests the role may be a figurehead position in an administration that doesn't prioritize governance. Meanwhile, the AI industry is moving at breakneck speed, and state attorneys general are not waiting for federal regulation — they're already exercising authority over AI use on their own.

The gap between what experts say we need (robust federal AI governance) and what we have (a leaderless institute with an acting director) is widening by the day.

## China Considers Tighter Export Controls on AI Models and Chips

**China is considering tighter export controls on AI models and chips, according to a Financial Times report. The move comes as Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 model rattles Silicon Valley, and just days after the Trump administration revived its own push to ban Chinese AI models in the US. The global AI supply chain is being reshaped by reciprocal restrictions from both sides.**

### What's happening

- The Financial Times reported that China is considering expanding its export controls to cover AI models and advanced chips
- This comes as Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 — a 2.8 trillion parameter open-weights model — tops benchmark charts and sends shockwaves through the US tech industry
- The Trump administration is simultaneously pushing to ban Chinese AI models in the US, citing cybersecurity concerns
- Tom's Hardware reported that while the US wants to ban Kimi K3, its open-weights availability makes an outright ban nearly impossible to enforce
- China is also forming the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization, positioning itself as the convener of international AI standards — a role the US has traditionally played
- The Verge ran a piece titled "China delivers a one-two punch to America's AI dominance," capturing the mood in Silicon Valley
- South China Morning Post reported that Anthropic is calling for the US to extend its AI lead, adding corporate pressure to the geopolitical dynamics

### Why it matters

We're watching the bifurcation of the global AI ecosystem in real time. Both sides are reaching for export controls, but the asymmetry is striking:

- **China's potential export controls** target the things the US depends on: rare earth minerals, advanced manufacturing, and now potentially AI model weights
- **US potential bans** target Chinese models that are already downloadable as open weights — making enforcement nearly impossible

The Kimi K3 situation illustrates the fundamental problem with banning open-weights software. You can sanction a company, but you can't sanction a GitHub repository. The weights are already out there. As Tom's Hardware noted, "downloadable open weights could make an outright US ban nearly impossible to enforce amid growing adoption."

Meanwhile, China's formation of the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization is a soft-power play that mirrors its approach to other international institutions: create an alternative governance structure, invite global participation, and set the standards. The US response — a leaderless safety institute and talk of bans — is not competing on the same field.

## Kimi K3: The Chinese AI Model That Spooked Silicon Valley

**Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 — a 2.8 trillion parameter model — has topped benchmark charts and sent Silicon Valley into a frenzy. The model outperforms Claude Fable 5 on Frontend Code Arena and will be released as open weights on Hugging Face by July 27. An OpenAI executive's comments about it kicked off a heated US tech debate about competition, open weights, and national security.**

### The story so far

- Moonshot AI released Kimi K3, a 2.8T parameter model that benchmarks at or above frontier level on multiple tests
- It beat Claude Fable 5 on Frontend Code Arena — a benchmark dominated by Western models until now
- The model will be published as open weights on Hugging Face by July 27, making it freely downloadable
- An OpenAI executive's comments about Kimi K3 kicked off a big US tech debate about whether Chinese AI models are competitive, whether open weights change the game, and whether bans can work
- Bloomberg reports that Kimi K3 "may be more about memory than compute" — suggesting architectural innovations rather than brute-force scaling
- Fortune compared the market impact to a "second DeepSeek shock"
- CNBC noted that "Chinese AI has leveled up, and brought renewed focus on the open weight model shift"
- Moonshot AI is planning a Hong Kong IPO, riding the wave of Kimi K3's success

### Why it matters

Kimi K3 is significant not because it's better than every Western model (it isn't — it's competitive on some benchmarks), but because of what it represents:

1. **Chinese labs are no longer catching up — they're releasing in parallel.** Kimi K3 wasn't a derivative of a Western model. It's an original architecture that benchmarks competitively.
2. **Open weights are the weapon of choice.** By releasing as open weights, Moonshot AI ensures the model can't be contained by any ban. It also commoditizes frontier intelligence and undercuts Western labs' pricing power.
3. **The memory-vs-compute angle is fascinating.** If Bloomberg is right that Kimi K3's performance comes from architectural innovation (better memory management) rather than raw compute, it means China is finding ways around US chip restrictions through smarter design.

The Silicon Valley anxiety is real. When The Verge says "China delivers a one-two punch to America's AI dominance," it's capturing a genuine shift in sentiment. The assumption that the US would maintain a comfortable lead in frontier AI is being challenged — not because China has surpassed it, but because the gap has narrowed to the point where it no longer feels safe.

- ADWEEK previously reported that OpenAI's ad business is on pace to miss its own forecast by 90%

- Morningstar noted that the revenue misses make a 2026 IPO unlikely
- Despite these misses, OpenAI continues to raise money and build infrastructure — the Stargate project alone is reportedly a $1.4 trillion commitment

### Why it matters

There's a growing gap between OpenAI's narrative dominance and its financial reality. The company that defined the AI moment, that made "ChatGPT" a verb, that raised the biggest private round in history — can't hit its own revenue targets. The ad business missing by 90% is particularly telling: it means brands aren't yet convinced that AI chat interfaces are the next Google.

This matters beyond OpenAI. If the company with the strongest brand, the most users, and the most funding can't hit its numbers, what does that say about the revenue potential of the entire industry? The bull case has always been "AI will be bigger than search, bigger than social, bigger than cloud." The bear case is starting to look more plausible: AI is incredible technology, but the business model is still being figured out.

Meanwhile, Chinese labs are commoditizing frontier intelligence by releasing open-weights models. If Kimi K3 and Qwen 3.8 are competitive with GPT-5.5 for free, the pricing power that OpenAI's revenue model depends on erodes further. This is the squeeze: rising costs on one side (infrastructure, talent, compute) and falling prices on the other (commoditized open weights).

## Jeff Bezos and UK Government Invest $450M in CuspAI

**Jeff Bezos and the UK government have led a $450 million Series B round in CuspAI, a British startup that uses AI to discover new materials for chip manufacturing. Nvidia and Meta are also involved in the partnership. The total valuation reportedly hits £2 billion ($2.6 billion).**

### What's happening

- CuspAI raised $450 million in a Series B round with participation from the Bezos Earth Fund and the UK government's Sovereign AI fund
- The startup uses AI to discover and design new materials specifically for chip manufacturing — directly addressing the materials science bottleneck in semiconductor production
- Nvidia is partnering with CuspAI, creating a direct link between the AI materials discovery platform and the world's largest chip designer
- The £2 billion valuation makes CuspAI one of the most valuable AI-for-science startups in the world
- The UK government's involvement signals a strategic interest in domestic AI capability for semiconductor independence

### Why it matters

This is the "AI for atoms" thesis getting serious backing. While most AI investment goes to software and services, CuspAI is targeting the physical substrate of the entire industry: the materials used to make chips. If AI can accelerate materials discovery (a domain where traditional R&D timelines are measured in decades), the implications for the semiconductor supply chain — and by extension, for AI itself — are enormous.

The Bezos and UK government participation is also significant. Bezos has been quietly investing in AI infrastructure plays, and the UK government's Sovereign AI fund is making a strategic bet on domestic AI capability that goes beyond just funding startups — it's about building a national advantage in a critical supply chain.

And the Nvidia partnership? That's the real signal. When the company that makes the hardware everyone is fighting over partners with a startup that could make that hardware better, you're looking at a vertically integrated play that could reshape chip manufacturing from the bottom up.

## AI Company Hacked — Entirely by an AI

**An AI company was hit by a hack that was entirely carried out by artificial intelligence, The Independent reported. This adds to the growing evidence that autonomous AI-driven attacks have moved from theoretical to operational — following on the heels of the Hugging Face incident disclosed last week.**

### What's known

- The Independent reported on an AI company that was hacked entirely by an artificial intelligence system
- This follows last week's Hugging Face disclosure of an autonomous AI-driven intrusion
- The details are still emerging, but the pattern is consistent: AI agents are being used to find vulnerabilities, exploit them, and move laterally across systems at machine speed
- This is the second publicly documented case of an AI-driven autonomous attack on an AI company in two weeks

### Why it matters

Two AI-driven autonomous attacks on AI companies in two weeks is no longer a coincidence — it's a pattern. The Hugging Face attack was described as the "first known" AI-driven autonomous intrusion. This second incident confirms that the attack method is replicable and being adopted.

The asymmetric warfare angle is stark. Attackers using AI face no guardrails, no usage policies, no ethical constraints. Defenders using commercial AI tools face all of those restrictions — as Hugging Face discovered when safety guardrails blocked their forensic analysis. The attacker's AI can study exploit payloads, craft attack chains, and probe defenses 24/7. The defender's AI refuses to look at the evidence.

We're entering an era where AI-on-AI warfare isn't a metaphor — it's the literal attack surface. And the current guardrail architecture, designed to prevent AI from helping humans do bad things, is inadvertently preventing AI from helping humans stop bad things.

## Over Half of US Employees Now Use AI at Work

**A Gallup survey, reported by US News, finds that more than half of US employees now use AI at work. But here's the catch: most of them are doing it without formal training, without company policies, and often without telling their employers. The AI adoption curve has gone vertical — the governance curve hasn't moved.**

### The data

- Gallup's survey finds that over 50% of US employees now use AI in some capacity at work
- This is up significantly from previous surveys, showing accelerating adoption
- The survey also found that workers who use AI are less likely to plan to stay with their current employer long-term
- Many employees are using AI tools without formal company policies or training — the "shadow AI" problem
- There's a growing gap between official AI adoption (companies rolling out tools and training) and unofficial adoption (employees using AI on their own)

### Why it matters

The majority of US workers using AI is a milestone moment, but the subtext is more important than the headline. The Gallup survey also found that AI users are less likely to stay with their employers — suggesting that AI exposure is creating a new class of mobile, skilled workers who see themselves as working *with* AI, not *for* companies that don't understand it.

The "shadow AI" problem is the real governance crisis. When over half your workforce is using AI tools and most of them aren't telling you, you don't have an adoption problem — you have a visibility problem. Company data is being pasted into ChatGPT, proprietary code is being debugged by Claude, and customer information is being summarized by Gemini — and most companies have no idea. The governance infrastructure is years behind the adoption curve, and the gap is widening.

## Gartner: AI Platform and Model Spending to Hit $64 Billion in 2026

**Gartner forecasts that worldwide spending on AI models and platforms will reach $64 billion in 2026, a 63% increase from 2025. The money is flowing to foundation models, cloud AI platforms, and the infrastructure layer that makes AI work at scale.**

### The forecast

- Gartner projects AI model and platform spending of $64 billion in 2026, up 63% year-over-year
- Foundation models are the biggest spending category, followed by cloud AI platforms
- The spending surge is driven by enterprise adoption, agentic AI deployments, and the ongoing infrastructure buildout
- This spending doesn't include the broader AI ecosystem (hardware, services, etc.) — just models and platforms

### Why it matters

$64 billion is a staggering number, and the 63% growth rate confirms that we're still in the early exponential phase of AI spending. But here's the tension: if OpenAI — the market leader — is missing its revenue targets by a "vast margin," who is capturing this spending?

The answer is likely that the spending is distributing more broadly than expected. Cloud providers (AWS, Azure, GCP) are capturing platform spending. Open-weights models are capturing model usage without capturing equivalent revenue. And a lot of the spending is going to infrastructure (TSMC's $100B Arizona expansion, data center buildouts) rather than to model providers.

The money is flowing, but it's not flowing to where the narratives say it should. That's a signal worth watching.

## Google's AI Search Is Imperiling the Open Web

**The New York Times published a major piece on how Google's AI-powered search is imperiling the open web. As AI overviews replace traditional search results, publishers are seeing traffic collapse while Google keeps users on its own properties. The symbiotic relationship between Google and the open web is breaking down.**

### What's happening

- The NYT piece documents how Google's AI overviews are replacing traditional blue links with AI-generated summaries
- Publishers across the web are reporting significant traffic declines as users get answers directly from Google without clicking through
- The fundamental economic model of the open web — publishers create content, Google sends traffic, publishers monetize through ads — is being disrupted
- Google argues that AI overviews actually increase engagement, but publishers' data tells a different story
- Common Sense Media recently called Google's AI search design an "unacceptable risk" for students

### Why it matters

This is the slow-motion crisis that's been building since Google launched AI overviews. The web's content ecosystem depends on a simple bargain: you create content, Google sends you traffic, you make money from that traffic. AI overviews break that bargain by answering the question on Google's own page, removing the need to visit the source.

If publishers can't monetize their content, they stop creating it. If they stop creating it, AI models have less training data. If AI models have less training data, they degrade. It's a negative feedback loop that undermines both the open web and the AI systems that depend on it. The NYT piece is the most prominent documentation yet of a problem that's been building for months.
