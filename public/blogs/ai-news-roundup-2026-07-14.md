## Anthropic Disables Fable 5 and Mythos 5 After US Export-Control Order, Then Gets Relief

**In one of the most dramatic regulatory episodes in AI history, Anthropic was forced to disable its most powerful models — Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 — after the US government issued an export-control directive barring the company from providing access to foreign nationals. Days later, the Trump administration lifted the restrictions on Fable 5 and Mythos 5 specifically, but the episode exposed the fragility of AI companies operating at the intersection of geopolitics and technology.**

### What's happening

- The US government ordered Anthropic to disable access to its most powerful models for all foreign nationals, Reuters reported
- Anthropic complied immediately, pulling Fable 5 and Mythos 5 from availability — affecting users worldwide, not just in restricted countries
- Fortune reported that Anthropic "disables Fable and Mythos AI models after US government bars it from giving foreigners access"
- Time Magazine covered the story with the headline: "Anthropic Pulls Its Most Powerful AI Models After U.S. Bars Foreign Access"
- Al Jazeera reported that the US "ordered Anthropic to disable AI models for all foreign nationals" — a sweeping order that went beyond typical export controls
- Within days, CNBC reported that "Anthropic says Trump admin has lifted export controls on Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5"
- Nikkei Asia confirmed the lifting of restrictions, but noted that the broader export-control framework remains in place
- Amazon, which has invested billions in Anthropic, had voiced concerns about Anthropic's AI models before the US crackdown, a Reuters source revealed — suggesting the company had advance knowledge of the regulatory direction
- The National Law Review noted that the episode raises "significant questions about the government's ability to restrict AI model access"
- Forbes provided a detailed timeline: Anthropic disabled the models, faced backlash from international users, then received relief — but the precedent was set
- CIO reported that Anthropic's new privacy policy "offers US consumers a way around the Fable ban" — a workaround that raised questions about whether the restrictions were meaningful

### Why it matters

This is the most consequential regulatory action in AI history, and it happened in the span of about a week. The US government demonstrated that it can order an AI company to disable its flagship product for all foreign nationals — not just enemies, not just restricted entities, but everyone outside US borders. That's an extraordinary assertion of power. The quick reversal suggests the government realized it had overreached, but the damage is done: every AI company now knows that the US can shut down their international access with a single order. And Anthropic, the company that built its entire brand on being globally responsible, was the first to comply without visible resistance. Amazon's advance concerns are particularly telling — they suggest that the companies closest to Anthropic saw this coming and tried to influence the outcome before it became public. The broader question is whether AI models should be treated like weapons technology, subject to export controls that prevent proliferation. If the answer is yes, then this week was just the beginning of a much longer regulatory journey. If the answer is no, then the US government just demonstrated that it can regulate AI as if it were weapons technology regardless of what anyone thinks. Either way, the era of borderless AI access is over.

## Over 200 Economists and Nobel Laureates Warn of AI's Economic Impact

**More than 200 economists, Nobel laureates, and technology leaders signed a rare joint statement warning that the world is not prepared for AI's economic impact. The letter, covered by Reuters, The New York Times, and Al Jazeera, calls for immediate policy action to address job displacement, economic inequality, and social disruption.**

### What's happening

- Reuters reported that "Nobel laureates are among more than 200 experts urging action on AI's economic impact"
- The New York Times led with: "Nearly 200 Economists and Tech Leaders Warn of A.I. Threats"
- Al Jazeera's headline captured the urgency: "Hundreds of experts warn the world must prepare now for AI's impact"
- Business Insider reported that the letter was signed by Eric Schmidt, Reid Hoffman, and other prominent figures from both economics and technology
- The warning comes amid growing evidence that AI is already displacing workers in specific industries, particularly in knowledge work, customer service, and content creation
- The signatories argue that while previous technological revolutions unfolded over decades, AI's impact is accelerating on a timeline of months to years
- The Vatican is separately convening Nobel laureates, world leaders, and AI experts at Borgo Laudato si' to discuss AI governance — signaling that the concern has reached the highest levels of global leadership
- UN News documented a parallel push for AI governance, noting that the UN Secretary-General "welcomed the first global AI assessment" and called for robust governance frameworks
- The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in the UK published its Mills Review on AI in financial services, recommending new regulatory frameworks for agentic AI in finance

### Why it matters

When 200 economists and Nobel laureates agree on something, it's usually worth paying attention. When they're joined by tech leaders who are building the very technology they're warning about, it's impossible to ignore. The letter doesn't say AI is inherently bad — it says the world is unprepared for the speed and scale of its economic impact. That's a different argument than "AI will destroy jobs" (which is reductive) or "AI will create more jobs than it destroys" (which may be true but misses the transition costs). The point is about preparedness: do we have the social safety nets, retraining programs, and policy frameworks to handle millions of displaced workers? The answer, clearly, is no. The Vatican's involvement is significant because it signals that this isn't just a Silicon Valley problem — it's a human problem. And the FCA's Mills Review shows that regulators are already thinking about how to govern AI agents in finance, which is one of the sectors most vulnerable to rapid automation. The question isn't whether AI will reshape the economy. The question is whether we'll be ready when it does.

## Tencent HY3 Tops OpenRouter Charts as Chinese AI Models Surge

**Tencent's HY3 model topped OpenRouter's usage charts, maintaining its position even after the free trial period ended. The model, which uses a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture with 295B total parameters but only 21B active per inference, represents a new efficiency paradigm in AI — and it's Chinese.**

### What's happening

- Tech Times reported that "Tencent HY3 Tops OpenRouter Charts" — a remarkable achievement for a Chinese model on a US-dominated platform
- VentureBeat noted that "Tencent's Apache-licensed Hy3 takes on GLM-5.2 at half the size — and wins everywhere except coding"
- Tencent open-sourced HY3 under the Apache license, making it freely available for commercial use
- The model uses MoE routing to activate only 21B parameters per inference from a total of 295B, achieving frontier-level performance at a fraction of the cost
- HY3 maintained strong usage even after the free trial period ended, suggesting genuine preference rather than just price shopping
- Tech Times also covered how "Base URL Swap Runs It Free in Codex CLI" — developers can easily switch to HY3 in their existing tools
- GIGAZINE reported that HY3 "maintained the top spot in OpenRouter's usage rankings for three weeks, and remains in second place even after the free trial period ended"
- Pandaily's deep dive asked "Has Tencent AI Finally Turned the Corner?" — a question that reflects years of Tencent struggling to compete in the AI model race

### Why it matters

Chinese AI models are no longer catching up — they're competing at the frontier, and in some cases, winning on efficiency. HY3's success on OpenRouter is significant because OpenRouter is where US developers go to find the best model for their task. If Chinese models are topping those charts, it means US developers are choosing them — not because of ideology, but because of performance-per-dollar. The MoE architecture is the key: by activating only 21B of 295B parameters per inference, HY3 achieves something that dense models can't — frontier quality at a fraction of the compute cost. This is the same approach that DeepSeek used to punch above its weight, and it's spreading. The open-source Apache license is also a strategic move: it removes the last barrier to adoption for companies that might be wary of Chinese technology. The message is clear — Tencent is not trying to control the ecosystem; it's trying to be part of it. Whether US regulators will allow that participation to continue is another question entirely.

## JadePuffer: The First Documented Agentic Ransomware Marks a New Era of Cyber Threats

**Security researchers at Sysdig documented the first-ever agentic ransomware attack, dubbed "JadePuffer," in which an AI agent autonomously conducted a ransomware attack. The attack used Langflow, an AI workflow tool, to execute the attack chain without direct human intervention — marking a significant escalation in AI-powered cyber threats.**

### What's happening

- Sysdig reported the first documented case of agentic ransomware, named "JadePuffer"
- Infosecurity Magazine covered the discovery: "Researchers Claim First Fully Agentic Ransomware: JadePuffer"
- Tech Times provided context: "Agentic Ransomware Is Real and Getting Cheaper: What Comes After JadePuffer"
- The attack used an AI agent built on Langflow to autonomously execute a ransomware attack chain
- Business Insider noted that this is "the first documented case" — meaning it's the first time researchers have confirmed that an AI agent conducted a ransomware attack without direct human control
- CyberScoop reported that "Sysdig clocks first documented case of agentic ransomware"
- AI Magazine went deeper: "Behind JadePuffer: The First Agentic AI Ransomware"
- SecurityWeek reported that the "Agentic AI Used to Conduct Ransomware Attack via Langflow"
- TechCrunch offered a dose of reality: "The 'first' AI-run ransomware attack still needed a human" — noting that while the attack was largely autonomous, human setup was still required

### Why it matters

JadePuffer is the moment the cybersecurity community has been dreading since ChatGPT launched. We've known that AI could be used to write phishing emails and generate malware. But this is different — an AI agent that autonomously executes a ransomware attack chain, making decisions and taking actions without human intervention. The TechCrunch caveat is important: "still needed a human." But that's cold comfort. The Wright Brothers' first flight only lasted 12 seconds, and it still changed the world. JadePuffer is the Wright Brothers moment for AI-powered cybercrime. It's rough, it's limited, and it still needs human setup. But it proves the concept works, and it will only get better. The "getting cheaper" part of the Tech Times headline is what should keep CISOs up at night. As AI models get cheaper and more capable, the cost of launching an autonomous cyberattack will approach zero. We're not there yet, but JadePuffer shows the trajectory.

This week was defined by borders — who gets to use AI, who controls it, and who gets shut out. Google is rebuilding its flagship model from scratch. Cursor and SpaceXAI are teaming up. And OpenClaw is becoming a nonprofit to keep the agent ecosystem neutral.

The AI industry in July 2026 looks like the semiconductor industry in the 1980s: massive capital investment, fierce international competition, government intervention, and a growing sense that whoever controls the infrastructure controls the future. The difference is speed — what took the semiconductor industry decades is happening in months. The borders being drawn today will define the AI landscape for years to come. The question is whether those borders will be drawn by governments, by companies, or by the technology itself.
